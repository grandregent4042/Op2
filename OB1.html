PERSONALITY IN ORGANISATIONAL BEHAVIOUR

Understanding Meaning, Determinants, Theories & Organisational Applications

Meaning & Definitions - Part 1

📖 DEFINITIONS & PERSPECTIVES
PERSONALITY DEFINED
Classical Definitions
Gordon Allport
"Dynamic psychophysical organisation" - Emphasizes the active, integrated nature of personality and its physiological basis
Stephen Robbins
"Sum of reactions and interactions" - Focuses on observable behaviors and patterns in various situations
Raymond Cattell
"Predictor of situational action" - Views personality as a tool to forecast behavior across contexts
What is Personality?
Concept: Stable set of psychological characteristics and behavioral patterns
Expression: Manifested in how we think, feel, and behave consistently
Uniqueness: Each person has a distinctive personality profile

Meaning & Characteristics - Part 2

✓ CORE CHARACTERISTICS OF PERSONALITY
Key Features
Stable & Enduring
Consistent patterns over time, though can evolve gradually with experience and development
Unique & Distinctive
Each individual has a unique combination of traits that distinguish them from others
Measurable
Can be assessed through various scientifically validated instruments and methods
Nature & Nurture
Result of both genetic inheritance and environmental influences throughout life
Dynamic & Adaptive
While relatively stable, personality can adapt to new situations and life experiences
Integrated System
All aspects work together as a unified whole affecting thoughts, feelings, and behaviors

Determinants of Personality - Part 1

🧬 BIOLOGICAL FACTORS (NATURE)
GENETIC INFLUENCES
Genetic Components
Genetic Structure
Chromosomes carry genetic information that influences personality traits, temperament, and behavioral predispositions
Brain Structure & Hormones
Neurological architecture and hormonal levels (neurotransmitters) affect emotional reactivity and social behavior
Twin Studies Evidence
Research shows identical twins share more personality similarities than fraternal twins, supporting genetic influence
Inborn Temperament
Natural behavioral predispositions present from infancy (activity level, irritability, sociability)
🌍 ENVIRONMENTAL FACTORS (NURTURE)
Environmental Influences
Cultural Context
Hofstede's cultural dimensions shape personality expression and values across societies and organizations
Family & Parenting
Parenting styles, family dynamics, and childhood relationships fundamentally shape personality development

Determinants of Personality - Part 2

👥 SOCIAL LEARNING & SITUATIONAL FACTORS
Social Learning Processes
Social Learning
Bandura's theory emphasizes learning through observation, modeling, and reinforcement from others in social contexts
Early Childhood Experiences
Critical periods in development where experiences significantly influence personality formation and behavior patterns
Peer Influence
Interactions with peers, friends, and social groups shape personality development throughout life stages
Situational Interaction
Strong Situations: Constrain personality expression (formal meetings, military training, court)
Weak Situations: Allow free personality expression (casual gatherings, creative tasks)
Interactionism: Behavior results from interaction between personality and environmental context (Mischel)

Theories - Trait Models - Part 1

📊 TRAIT THEORIES
TRAIT APPROACH
Big Five Model (OCEAN)
Openness to Experience
Curiosity, creativity, appreciation for art, imagination. High scorers seek variety and novelty
Conscientiousness
Organization, responsibility, dependability. Strongest predictor of job performance
Extraversion
Sociability, assertiveness, outgoingness. Predicts leadership emergence and networking ability
Agreeableness
Cooperation, compassion, empathy. Predicts teamwork quality and collaboration effectiveness
Neuroticism
Emotional stability, anxiety, stress sensitivity. Affects workplace emotional interactions

Theories - Other Models - Part 2

📊 OTHER TRAIT MODELS
Cattell's 16 Personality Factors (16PF)
Comprehensive: 16 distinct personality factors derived through factor analysis
Detailed: More granular than Big Five for specific organizational assessments
Eysenck's PEN Model
Three Dimensions
Psychoticism: Aggression, impulsivity
Extraversion
Sociability, dominance, assertiveness
Neuroticism
Emotional stability, anxiety levels
🧠 PSYCHODYNAMIC THEORY (FREUD)
Structure of Personality
Id
Unconscious, instinctual desires and primitive urges. Seeks immediate gratification without restraint
Ego
Reality-based mediator between id and superego. Makes rational decisions balancing demands
Superego
Moral conscience incorporating values, ethics, and societal rules learned from parents and society

Theories - Social Learning & Humanistic - Part 3

👥 SOCIAL LEARNING THEORY (BANDURA)
Key Concepts
Self-Efficacy
Belief in one's ability to succeed in specific situations. Determines effort and persistence in tasks
Reciprocal Determinism
Three-way interaction: Person (traits) ↔ Behavior ↔ Environment influence each other continuously
Observational Learning
People learn personality traits and behaviors by watching others (modeling) and observing consequences
🌟 HUMANISTIC THEORIES
Growth-Oriented Approach
Maslow: Self-actualisation as highest motivation - personality grows toward full potential
Rogers: Self-concept and congruence - personality healthy when real self matches ideal self
Humanistic Perspective
Growth Potential
Individuals have innate capacity for growth, self-improvement, and self-determination
Authenticity & Congruence
Healthy personality emerges when actual self aligns with ideal self, reducing internal conflict

OB Attributes - Specific Traits - Part 1

🎯 SPECIFIC PERSONALITY ATTRIBUTES
OB-RELEVANT TRAITS
Control & Behavior Attributes
Locus of Control
Internal: Believes in personal control → higher performance, motivation
External: Believes in fate/others → different motivation patterns
Machiavellianism
Manipulative, pragmatic approach to politics and power. High = cunning, deceptive; Low = straightforward, ethical
Self-Monitoring
Ability to adjust behavior to fit social situations. High = adaptable; Low = consistent across contexts
Type A vs Type B
Type A: Competitive, impatient, aggressive
Type B: Relaxed, patient, easygoing
MBTI Dichotomies
16 types from E/I, S/N, T/F, J/P combinations for organizational team dynamics and role matching

OB Outcomes - Work Impact - Part 1

💼 PERSONALITY IMPACT ON WORK BEHAVIOR
ORGANISATIONAL OUTCOMES
Performance & Leadership
Job Performance
Conscientiousness strongest predictor of task performance; Agreeableness impacts teamwork quality
Leadership Emergence
Extraversion and dominance traits predict who emerges as leader in groups and teams
Teamwork & Cooperation
Agreeableness predicts collaboration; Openness supports diverse perspectives and innovation
Creativity & Innovation
Openness to experience strongest correlate with creative output and innovative problem-solving

Measurement & Ethics - Methods - Part 1

🔍 MEASUREMENT METHODS
ASSESSMENT APPROACHES
Assessment Tools
Self-Report Inventories
Questionnaires where individuals rate themselves (Big Five, MBTI, NEO-PI). Most common in organizations
Projective Tests
TAT (Thematic Apperception Test), Rorschach - Indirect assessment interpreting responses to ambiguous stimuli
Behavioral Observation
Systematic observation of actual behavior in natural settings or assessment center simulations
AI & Neurobiological Methods
Emerging technologies: facial coding, voice analysis, neural imaging for more objective assessment

Measurement & Ethics - Challenges - Part 2

⚖️ CHALLENGES & ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS
Key Challenges
Social Desirability Bias
Respondents answer to portray themselves positively rather than honestly. Reduces assessment validity
Cultural Validity Gap
Assessment instruments developed in Western contexts may not be valid across different cultures and values
AI Privacy Concerns
Neurobiological and algorithmic personality assessment raises privacy and discrimination concerns
Ethical Safeguards Required
Informed consent, confidentiality, fairness, transparency in purpose, and proper interpretation essential
Organisational Best Practices
Validation: Use only empirically validated instruments for specific purposes
Transparency: Clear communication about assessment use and data handling to participants
Fairness: Ensure assessments don't discriminate against protected groups; avoid stereotyping
Professional Administration: Trained professionals interpret results; avoid self-diagnosis or online misuse
