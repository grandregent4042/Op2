🤝 INDUSTRIAL RELATIONS: MEANING, NATURE & SCOPE
Definitions & Frameworks
IR
DEFINITIONS
ILO Definition IR deals with relationship between state, employers, and workers organizations; Emphasizes tripartite structure
J.T. Dunlop (1958) IR is set of rules governing employment and ways rules are made/changed by actors (management, labour, government) within technology, market, power contexts
V.B. Singh IR concerned with systems, rules, procedures used by unions and employers to determine reward for effort and employment conditions
Functional Definition IR = complex inter-relationships between three actors — Management (employers), Labour (employees/unions), State (government/law) — mediated by rules governing employment
NATURE OF IR
Dynamic System Not static; constantly evolving; Rules and relationships change over time
Tripartite Three key players: Management, Labour, State; Each brings own interests and power
Rule-Governed Employment relationship regulated by formal rules (legislation, contracts) and informal rules (customs, practices)
Conflict-Prone Inherent tension between capital's profit motive and labour's welfare; Conflicts natural in IR system
SCOPE OF IR
Collective Bargaining Unions negotiate with employers on wages, hours, conditions; Central IR mechanism
Dispute Resolution Mechanisms to handle strikes, lockouts, conflicts; Conciliation, arbitration, adjudication
Worker Participation Worker voice in management decisions; WPCs, joint committees, works councils
Legal Framework Labour legislation, regulations, codes governing employment; Industrial Disputes Act, Labour Codes
IMPORTANCE OF IR
Productivity & Growth Harmonious IR reduces disputes, absenteeism, turnover; Directly raises productivity
Industrial Peace Strikes cause massive output losses; Conciliation/arbitration contain conflict
Worker Welfare Fair IR ensures just wages, safe conditions, protection from arbitrary treatment
Economic Development Industrialization depends on harmonious IR; FDI requires stable labour relations
IR CORE CONCEPT
System Approach: IR shaped by actors, context (technology, market, power), and ideology
Three Dimensions: Rules (formal + informal), Actors (Mgmt, Labour, State), Process (bargaining, dispute resolution, participation)

5️⃣ FIVE APPROACHES TO INDUSTRIAL RELATIONS
Theoretical Frameworks (PYQ 2022 - 20M)
5 APPS
UNITARY APPROACH
View Organization as single integrated community; Everyone (management & workers) shares common goals
Assumption No inherent conflict of interest; Unions unnecessary; Conflict is aberration, not norm
Solution Eliminate unions; Strengthen management control; Direct worker-management communication
Problem Ignores real interests divergence; Naive about power dynamics; Exploitative without checks
PLURALISTIC APPROACH
View Organizations have diverse interest groups; Management, workers, unions all legitimate actors with different goals
Assumption Conflict inherent and normal; Must be managed, not eliminated; Interests reconcilable through negotiation
Solution Collective bargaining; Joint committees; Compromise and negotiation between parties
Strength Realistic about conflict; Protects workers; Gives voice to labour; Mainstream approach
MARXIST APPROACH
View IR reflects class struggle between capital and labour; Fundamental conflict of interests (surplus value extraction)
Assumption Capital exploits labour inherently; System structurally inequal; Workers must unite against exploitation
Solution Revolutionary change; Worker control; Overthrow capitalist system; Temporary stage to communism
Problem Revolutionary vision unrealistic; Ignores pragmatic accommodation possible; Polarizing
SYSTEMS APPROACH
View (Dunlop) IR as dynamic system with inputs (actors, context), conversion process (rules), outputs (agreements, practices)
Elements Actors (workers, employers, govt), Context (technology, market, power distribution), Ideology (shared understandings)
Analysis System in equilibrium when actors and context aligned; Breakdown when misalignment (conflict arises)
Strength Holistic; Accounts for complexity; Explains how IR systems vary by country/industry
BEHAVIOURAL APPROACH
View Focus on individual and group behaviour in IR; Psychology of worker motivation, leadership, group dynamics
Analysis Study motivations, perceptions, attitudes, communication patterns; How these affect negotiations and conflicts
Solution Improve communication, understanding, human relations; Address psychological needs; Better leadership
Application Negotiation skills training; Conflict resolution; Improving workplace culture; Motivation programs
PYQ 2022 (20M) - FIVE APPROACHES
Question Focus: Compare 5 approaches; Explain which most appropriate for Indian context
Answer Strategy: Critique each; Show pluralistic + systems approach most practical for India

🏢 TRADE UNIONS: DEFINITIONS & REASONS FOR JOINING
Formation & Member Motivation
TUs
DEFINITIONS
Trade Unions Act 1926 Section 2(h): "Any combination, whether temporary or permanent, formed primarily for purpose of regulating relations between workmen and employers or workmen or employers"
Sidney & Beatrice Webb "Continuous association of wage-earners for purpose of maintaining/improving conditions of working lives" — Classic definition; cite in 20M answers
ILO Definition Organizations established to protect/promote interests of members through collective bargaining and influencing legislation
REASONS FOR JOINING (MOTIVATION)
Economic Protection Better wages, benefits, job security through collective bargaining; Individual workers have minimal power vs employers
Protection from Arbitrary Management Grievance procedures, job security provisions protect against unfair treatment; Collective voice against exploitation
Social Belonging Sense of community and solidarity; Particularly important in large, impersonal workplaces; Brotherhood/sisterhood
Political Voice Advocacy in political process; Influencing labour legislation, minimum wage, social security; Collective political power
Legal Protection Access to legal representation in workplace disputes; Union provides resources individual can't afford
Class Consciousness (Marxist View) Workers' solidarity against capital exploitation; Joining as act of class identification; Revolutionary potential
UNION FUNCTIONS
Collective Bargaining Negotiate wages, hours, working conditions with employers; Central function
Grievance Handling Represent members in disputes; Advocate for fair treatment; Present grievances
Advocacy Influence legislation; Campaign for worker-friendly policies; Public awareness
Member Services Training, education, welfare schemes, legal aid for members
UNION FORMATION PROCESS
Membership Must have at least 7 workmen (or 10% of workforce if larger); Can register with Registrar
Registration Union registers with government; Gains legal status; Rights and obligations
Recognition Employer recognizes union; Enables collective bargaining; Usually based on membership %
UNION AS COLLECTIVE POWER
Core Insight: Individual worker weak; Union creates collective strength to balance employer power
Economic + Social: Not just wages; Also dignity, voice, community, protection

⚖️ TRADE UNIONS ACT 1926 & RECOGNITION
Legislation & Recognition Criteria (PYQ 2022)
TU ACT
TU ACT 1926 - KEY PROVISIONS
Registration Requirement Unions can register with Registrar; Gains legal status and rights; Minimum 7 members or 10% workforce
Rights of Registered Unions Acquire property, sue/be sued; Open bank account; Enter into agreements; Legal personality
Protection Against Dismissal Officers of registered unions cannot be dismissed without prior government notification; Job protection
Immunity from Liability Union not liable for unlawful acts of members if done without authorization or in violation of union rules
Right to Collective Bargaining Registered unions have right to collectively bargain with employers; Legally recognized bargaining agent
RECOGNITION CRITERIA (PYQ 2022)
Membership Criteria Minimum 51% of employees must be members for exclusive recognition; or 30% for representative recognition
Financial Soundness Union must maintain proper accounts; Financial viability; No misappropriation
Democratic Functioning Regular elections; Transparent constitution; Accountability to members; No authoritarian control
Representativeness Actually represents workers; Responsive to member needs; Active engagement with members
Disputes Settlement Track record of peaceful dispute resolution; Not promoting violence or illegal actions
RECOGNITION RIGHTS
Collective Bargaining Recognized unions authorized to negotiate with management; Binding agreements
Information Access Right to information about company finances, policies affecting workers; Transparency
Worker Consultation Management consults union on major decisions; Representation in joint committees
Grievance Machinery Union participates in grievance resolution; Representation in dispute handling
NEW LABOUR CODES 2020 IMPACT
Code on IR 2020 Consolidates and amends TU Act, ID Act, other laws; Higher recognition thresholds (20% for negotiating); Affects union leverage
Stricter Norms More stringent criteria for recognition; Reduced immunity in some areas; Changes dispute settlement
Worker Concerns Critics argue codes weaken union position; Raise thresholds; Reduce collective bargaining power
RECOGNITION AS POWER BALANCE
Significance: Recognition gives union legal standing; Employer must negotiate; Workers have collective voice
Codes 2020: Higher thresholds make recognition harder; Affects union strength in newer workplaces

📣 TRADE UNION MOVEMENT IN INDIA
Historical Evolution & CTUOs
TU MOVEMENT
HISTORICAL PHASES
Early Phase (1880s-1920) Birth of organized labour; Initial strikes in textile mills, railways; Pre-independence; Nationalist consciousness
Organized Phase (1920-1947) AITUC founded 1920; Integration with freedom struggle; Link between labour and nationalist movements
Post-Independence (1947-1965) Constitution protects collective bargaining; Industrial Disputes Act 1947; Union growth; Multiple CTUOs emerge
Growth Phase (1965-1990) Union strength increases; Major strikes in 60s, 70s, 80s; Yellow unions emerge (government-aligned)
Liberalization Era (1991-Present) Union decline due to globalization, privatization, contractualization; New organizing in IT, retail, gig economy
CENTRAL TU ORGANIZATIONS (CTUOs)
AITUC All India Trade Union Congress; 1920; Oldest; Communist affiliation; Large membership; Political influence
INTUC Indian National Trade Union Congress; 1947; Affiliated Congress; Moderate approach; Large membership; Pan-India
BMS Bharatiya Mazdoor Sangh; 1955; RSS-affiliated; Nationalist ideology; Growing membership; Hindu nationalist base
CITU Centre of Indian Trade Unions; 1970; Communist (Marxist); Breakaway from AITUC; Strong in manufacturing
SEWA Self-Employed Women's Association; 1972; Informal sector focus; Women-centric; 2M+ members; Innovative model
CTUO CHARACTERISTICS
Political Affiliation Most CTUOs have political party links; AITUC-Communist, INTUC-Congress, BMS-RSS; Affects autonomy
Ideology Range from revolutionary (AITUC) to moderate (INTUC) to nationalist (BMS); Different approaches to struggles
Membership Competition CTUOs compete for membership; Duplicate organizing; Fragmentation; Reduces collective bargaining power
INDUSTRIAL COVERAGE
Formal Sector Strong Manufacturing, mining, railways, government; Well-organized; But declining with privatization
Informal Sector Weak Construction, agriculture, domestic work; Difficult to organize; SEWA exception; Huge untapped base
Emerging Sectors IT, retail, BPO, gig economy; Nascent union presence; New organizing models needed
INDIA'S TU MOVEMENT CHARACTER
Political: CTUOs tied to political parties; Reduces worker autonomy; Prioritizes politics over worker interests
Fragmented: Multiple CTUOs compete; Weakens collective power; Multiplicity problem
Sectoral Gap: Strong in formal sector; Weak in informal sector; Coverage imbalanced

⚠️ PROBLEMS OF TRADE UNIONS IN INDIA
10 Critical Issues (PYQ Frequent - Every Year)
PROBLEMS
PROBLEM 1-3
Multiplicity of TUs Root structural problem (PYQ 2023); Too many unions competing; Fragmentation; Reduces collective bargaining strength; Employers play one against another
Political Affiliation Most CTUOs linked to political parties; Politics dominates worker interests; Reduced autonomy; Competing ideologies weaken unity
Outside Leadership Many union leaders from outside industry; Not workers themselves; Don't understand ground realities; Promote personal agendas over worker needs
PROBLEM 4-6
Corruption & Mismanagement Union funds misused; Lack of transparency; Democratic deficits; Leaders not accountable; Members lose faith
Strike Culture Tendency toward strikes over negotiation; Rigid ideology; Confrontational approach; Damages competitiveness; Hurts workers economically
Weak Organization Poor administration; Irregular meetings; Inactive membership; Limited communication; Members uninformed; Services poor
PROBLEM 7-9
Low Union Density Only ~5% of workforce unionized; Declining membership; Formal sector coverage limited; Informal sector largely uncovered
Inability to Organize Informal Sector Construction, agriculture, domestic work unorganized; Workers highly vulnerable; Difficult working conditions; Powerless without collective voice
Inadequate Legal Protection While rights exist on paper, enforcement weak; Employers violate with impunity; Arbitration system slow; Remedies inadequate
PROBLEM 10 & OUTLOOK
Adaptation to Modern Economy Manufacturing declining; Service sector expanding; Gig economy emerging; Unions structured for factory; Struggle with service/informal models
Liberalization Pressure Post-1991 globalization; Multinational capital mobile; Outsourcing; Contract labour; Reduces union leverage; Casualization of workforce
Solutions Needed Unity (reduce multiplicity); Democracy (reduce outside control); Services (improve member benefit); Organizing (informal sector); Modern methods
MULTIPLICITY AS ROOT PROBLEM (PYQ 2023)
Challenge: Multiple CTUOs compete; Divide worker solidarity; Weaken collective power
Root Cause: Political affiliation; Ideological differences; Inadequate labour legislation
Impact: Employers choose which union to recognize; Workers lose negotiating power; Deepens IR conflicts

🌍 IMPACT OF LIBERALISATION ON TU MOVEMENT
Post-1991 Changes (PYQ 2022)
LIBZ
NEGATIVE IMPACTS (8 DIMENSIONS)
Casualization of Labour Shift to contract, temporary, gig workers; Not permanent employees; Difficult to unionize; Turnover high; No long-term commitment
Outsourcing & Privatization Functions outsourced to small contractors; Public sector privatized; Direct employment reduced; Workers fragmented across multiple employers
Multinational Capital Mobility MNCs can shift production across countries; Threatens to relocate if unions demand higher wages; Undermines union bargaining power
Global Competition Indian firms compete with low-cost producers globally; Pressure to cut labour costs; Resist wage increases; Union demands seen as uncompetitive
Decline of Manufacturing Traditional union base (factories, mines); Service sector expanding; Harder to organize white-collar workers; Membership declining
NEGATIVE IMPACTS (CONTINUED)
Weakened Legal Protections Labour Codes 2020 raise union recognition thresholds; Stricter rules; Reduced immunity; Harder for unions to achieve recognition
Employer Anti-Union Strategies Increased use of contract labour to avoid unionization; Threats of relocation; Speed of hiring/firing; Reduced incentive to organize
Union Membership Decline Unionization dropped from ~5% to lower; Young generation less interested; Service sector culture individualistic; Collective identity weak
POSITIVE/ADAPTIVE RESPONSES
Global Supply Chain Opportunities Export manufacturing, retail, logistics; New organizing frontiers; If unions adapt to new structures and sectors
International Standards Pressure Global buyers require ILO compliance; H&M, Nike responsible sourcing; External pressure for union recognition in supply chains
SEWA Model Innovation Self-employed women (2M+ members); Replicable for gig workers, informal sector; Shows new union forms possible
Service Sector Growth Banking, insurance, IT, healthcare expanding; New white-collar unions possible; Bank unions remain strong
Platform Worker Organizing IFAT representing Ola/Uber drivers; New frontier for unionism; Gig economy collective action emerging
REFORM AGENDA
Labour Codes Unfinished Implementation pending; Concerns about worker protections; Tripartite dialogue continues; Union demands for amendments
Informal Sector Inclusion Need to extend union protections to informal economy; 90% of workforce; Most vulnerable; Policy attention increasing
LIBERALIZATION PARADOX (PYQ 2022)
Challenge: Globalization fragmented union base; Casualization reduced permanent employment; Mobility weakened bargaining
But Opportunities: New sectors (services, IT), global standards, gig economy organizing; Adaptation required
Indian Response: SEWA innovation; Bank unions resilience; Platform worker organizing; But scale insufficient
