⚡ AUTHORITY, POWER & CONTROL
Weber's Typology & French & Raven's Bases
AUTHORITY & POWER
WEBER'S TYPOLOGY
Charismatic Authority Based on personal magnetism, inspiration, exceptional qualities; Followers believe in leader's exceptional powers
Traditional Authority Based on established customs, history, precedent; "We do it this way because we always have"
Rational-Legal Authority Based on formal position, written rules, rational procedures; Most common in modern organizations
KEY DISTINCTIONS
Authority Legitimate right to make decisions; Granted by organization; Can be taken away; Based on position
Power Ability to influence regardless of authority; Can be formal or informal; Hard to remove; Based on multiple sources
Accountability Being answerable for actions; Legal/moral liability; Cannot be delegated
Responsibility Obligation to perform assigned duties; Can be delegated; Voluntary commitment
FRENCH & RAVEN (5 BASES)
1. Legitimate Power Based on position/title; Formal authority granted by organization
2. Reward Power Ability to provide rewards (salary, promotion, recognition, bonuses)
3. Coercive Power Ability to punish (termination, demotion, reprimand, withholding benefits)
4. Expert Power Based on knowledge, skills, expertise, technical competence
5. Referent Power Based on personal appeal, charisma, likeability, admiration by followers
SPAN OF CONTROL
Graicunas (1933) As subordinates increase arithmetically, potential relationships increase geometrically
Example 5 subordinates = 100 relationships; 10 = 5,000+ (supervisor cognitive overload)
Wide Span Many subordinates per manager; Flat structure; Lower coordination; Risk of overload
Narrow Span Few subordinates per manager; Tall structure; Higher coordination; More management layers
POWER EFFECTIVENESS RANKING
Most Effective: Expert & Referent power - self-motivating, builds commitment
Moderately Effective: Legitimate power - formal right, but depends on culture
Least Effective: Coercive power - creates resentment, resistance, hidden compliance

📊 LINE & STAFF FUNCTIONS
Comparison & Causes of Conflict (PYQ 2022)
LINE & STAFF
LINE FUNCTIONS
Definition Direct responsibility for achieving organizational objectives
Examples Production, Sales, Operations, Customer Service, Distribution
Characteristics Authority to make decisions, Direct impact on outcomes, Accountable for results, Action-oriented
Orientation Speed, results, implementation, bottom-line impact
STAFF FUNCTIONS
Definition Advisory/support role without direct authority over line operations
Examples HR, Finance, Legal, Planning, Quality, R&D, Training, IT
Characteristics Limited decision authority, Indirect impact through advice, Accountable for quality of recommendations, Analysis-oriented
Orientation Thoroughness, caution, analysis, best practices, compliance, risk mitigation
CAUSES OF CONFLICT (PYQ 2022)
Orientation Clash Line = action-oriented (speed, results); Staff = analysis-oriented (thoroughness, caution) - fundamental attitudinal clash
Authority Ambiguity When staff authority creeps from advisory into directive without formal sanction - line feels bypassed & undermined
Age/Experience Mismatch Young MBA staff specialists advising seasoned line managers - generational tension ("what does this consultant know about our shop floor?")
Information Asymmetry Staff's privileged access to top mgmt & strategic data creates perceived favoritism that line resents
Credit-Blame Asymmetry Staff gets credit when advice succeeds; line bears blame if it fails - perceived as structurally unfair
Structural Ambiguity In functional authority, staff has limited command authority - violates Fayol's unity of command, creates dual loyalty
MINIMISE CONFLICT
Clarify Authority Define whether staff is purely advisory, functional authority (limited command), or concurring (must approve)
Joint Accountability Hold staff jointly accountable for OUTCOMES of recommendations, not just advice quality
Job Rotation Rotate managers between line & staff roles to build mutual understanding & empathy
Business Partner Model Modern evolution: Embed staff specialists within line operations (HR Business Partner, Finance BP) - shared accountability
Leadership Clarity Top mgmt must explicitly back staff recommendations OR clearly restrict staff to advisory role
CRITICAL PYQ 2022 INSIGHT
Core Issue: Fundamental attitudinal clash between orientations - no structural fix can fully eliminate this tension
Root Cause: Different operational philosophies - speed vs. caution, action vs. analysis, results vs. thoroughness
Answer Strategy: Acknowledge structural causes + psychological factors + practical solutions for minimizing (not eliminating) conflict

🏢 FUNCTIONAL STRUCTURE - THE TWO-EDGED SWORD
Design, Advantages & Limitations (PYQ 2016)
FUNCTIONAL STRUCT
DEFINITION & DESIGN
Structure Organization grouped by functions/specializations - Marketing, Finance, Production, HR, Operations
Reporting All people in function report to functional head; Functional head reports to CEO
Focus Depth of expertise in each function; Clear specialization; Functional excellence
ADVANTAGES
Expertise & Efficiency Deep specialization; Best practices within function; Economies of scale in shared resources
Cost Reduction No duplication of specialists; Centralized services; High utilization of experts
Career Development Clear career path within function; Expert advancement possible; Professional identity
Control & Coordination Clear authority lines; Functional head controls resources; Standardization within function
Simplicity Easy to understand; Clear responsibilities; Straightforward reporting structure
LIMITATIONS
Silo Mentality Each function focused on own goals; Limited cross-functional coordination; Suboptimization
Slow Response Cross-functional decisions require coordination through hierarchy; Sluggish adaptation to market changes
Poor Customer Focus Customer needs filtered through functions; No single point responsibility for customer satisfaction; Fragmented customer view
Product/Project Weakness Cannot effectively manage multiple products/projects; Responsibility diffused across functions
Coordination Overhead Many meetings needed; Committees for cross-functional issues; Management time consumed by coordination
TWO-EDGED SWORD (PYQ 2016)
Relative Advantages What is advantage in one context becomes disadvantage in another - hence "two-edged sword"
Efficiency Paradox Efficient within functions BUT inefficient overall due to coordination costs & silos
Suitable When Single product/service, stable environment, functional excellence critical, cost control essential
Problematic When Multiple products, fast-changing environment, customer responsiveness critical, innovation needed
PYQ 2016 CRITICAL CONCEPT
"Two-edged sword" implies: No single "best" structure - benefits come with costs
In exam answer: Discuss relative advantages (advantages relative to other structures) + identify context where advantages/disadvantages emerge
Example: Efficiency advantage → disadvantage if customer responsiveness needed more than cost control

📈 DIVISIONAL & MATRIX STRUCTURES
M-Form Design & Chandler's Thesis
DIVISIONAL & MATRIX
DIVISIONAL (M-FORM)
Definition Self-contained divisions organized by product, geography, or customer type - each with own functional resources (Production, Marketing, Finance, HR)
Chandler's Thesis (1962) "Structure follows strategy" - diversification strategy naturally leads to M-Form as functional structure cannot manage multiple distinct businesses
Advantages Strong P&L accountability; Customer/product focus; General management development; Competition between divisions; Flexibility to add/remove businesses
Disadvantages Duplication of functions (each division has HR, Finance) = loss of economies of scale; Divisional autonomy can fragment corporate identity; Internal competition can become zero-sum
India Examples Tata Group (Tata Motors, Tata Steel, TCS, Tata Consultancy); Mahindra Group (organized by business verticals)
MATRIX STRUCTURE
Definition Dual reporting: Employees report to BOTH functional manager AND project/product manager simultaneously
Frequency in UPSC Most frequent topic - 5 of last 11 years; High exam probability
When Adopted Dual focus (functional expertise + project coordination); Efficient specialist use; Complex interdependent projects; Two equal strategic priorities
MATRIX PROBLEMS
Unity of Command Violation Two bosses create role ambiguity, psychological stress, accountability confusion ("Which boss evaluates me?")
Power Struggles Functional & project managers compete for same people/resources - degenerates into political battles consuming management attention
Admin Cost More managers needed, more meetings required, more coordination overhead - expensive vs simpler structures
Decision Paralysis Consensus requirement between two authorities slows decisions - damaging in fast-moving environments
Employee Stress Constant negotiation between conflicting demands, ambiguous priorities, dual loyalty creates stress & burnout over time
Cultural Incompatibility Requires high tolerance for ambiguity & strong interpersonal skills - Indian orgs with high power-distance culture (Hofstede) struggle with dual authority ambiguity
CHANDLER'S "STRUCTURE FOLLOWS STRATEGY"
Core Insight: Organizational structure must be designed to support strategy - mismatch causes problems
Implication: Single-product firms use functional; Diversified firms use divisional (M-Form) - structure must match strategic scope
Application: When Tata diversified, functional structure became inadequate - led to M-Form divisional structure
MATRIX - UPSC FAVORITE (5 in 11 years)
High exam probability: Be very prepared on matrix structure - reasons, problems, solutions
Indian Context: Many Indian IT companies, pharma use matrix - relevant examples
Hofstede Relevance: India high power-distance makes matrix adoption challenging - discuss this cultural angle

🌐 NETWORK / VIRTUAL ORGANISATION - POST-INDUSTRIAL FORM
Design, Advantages & Limitations
NETWORK ORG
DEFINITION & DESIGN
Core Concept Core firm outsources most functions to network of specialist partners - connected by contracts, IT systems, and shared purpose
Boundaries Deliberately fuzzy organizational boundaries; What is "inside" vs "outside" the firm unclear
Coordination Through digital platforms, contracts, performance metrics, shared vision rather than hierarchy
Focus Core competencies retained; Non-core functions outsourced to specialists who do it better
ADVANTAGES
Maximum Flexibility Can quickly add/remove partners; Scale up/down based on demand; No legacy constraints
Low Fixed Costs Pay for services used; No large fixed asset/overhead base; Variable cost structure
Global Talent Access to best global specialists; Not limited to local hiring; Diverse expertise
Rapid Scaling Can scale operations quickly by adding partners; No infrastructure build-up needed
No Legacy Burden Not carrying old infrastructure, systems, or processes; Clean start each project
LIMITATIONS
Loss of Control Cannot directly control quality & culture of partner firms; Dependent on third parties
Coordination Complexity Managing network of partners across geographies, time zones, cultures; Integration challenges
IP Risk Intellectual property shared with partners; Risk of leakage, misuse, or competitors gaining access
Culture Coherence Difficult to build unified culture across network; Partners have own cultures & priorities
Accountability Diffusion Unclear who is responsible when problems occur; Contractual disputes common; Legal complexity
INDIA EXAMPLES
Flipkart Logistics through Ekart + third-party delivery partners; Marketplace model with sellers
Ola/Uber Driver-partners as network nodes; Platform connects drivers to customers; Minimal own assets
Pharma Companies Outsource manufacturing to contract manufacturers (CMOs); Focus on R&D & marketing
Fashion Brands Design in-house; Manufacturing outsourced; No own factories or infrastructure
NETWORK AS POST-INDUSTRIAL FORM
Shift: From manufacturing-centric (hierarchical control) to service/knowledge-centric (network coordination)
Enabled By: IT platforms, digital communication, global supply chains, gig economy, outsourcing maturity
Future Trend: More organizations adopting network model as traditional hierarchy limitations become apparent

🎭 POWER & POLITICS IN ORGANISATIONS
Sources, Political Behaviour & Management
POWER & POLITICS
SOURCES OF POWER
Formal Authority Official position; Right to make decisions; Legitimate power
Control of Resources Budget allocation, materials, personnel - whoever controls resources has power
Information Control Access to critical information; Knowledge asymmetries create power imbalances
Expertise Technical knowledge, skills, competencies valued by organization
Social Networks Relationships, connections, alliances, coalitions provide influence
Charisma/Personality Personal appeal, influence over others, referent power
POLITICAL BEHAVIOUR
Emerges From Ambiguous goals, Scarce resources, Significant decisions, Org change, Matrix structures (power ambiguity)
Positive Politics Building coalitions for beneficial change; Advocating for underrepresented interests; Facilitating stakeholder alignment
Negative Politics Withholding information for advantage; Scapegoating; Sabotaging rivals; Creating information silos; Empire-building
Management Response Transparency about resource allocation; Clear decision-rights frameworks; Performance-based culture rewarding outcomes over political skill
RELATIONSHIP TO STRUCTURE
Functional Structure Clear authority limits politics somewhat; But functional silos create competition for resources & influence
Matrix Structure High politics due to power ambiguity; Dual authority creates competing power centers; Political skills become survival requirement
Divisional Structure Inter-divisional politics over corporate resources; Competition can become destructive zero-sum game
Network Structure Less internal politics; But politics exists in partner relationships and partner selection
POLITICAL BEHAVIOUR MANAGEMENT STRATEGY
Not Eliminate: Politics is inevitable in organizations - cannot be eliminated, only managed
Structural Design: Reduce politics triggers through clear authority, transparent resource allocation, unambiguous goals
Cultural Approach: Foster results-oriented culture where political skill matters less than actual performance
Leadership: Model transparency, fairness, and merit-based decisions to discourage destructive political behavior

💻 IMPACT OF IT ON ORGANISATIONAL STRUCTURE
Technology-Driven Transformation
IT IMPACT
FLATTENING HIERARCHIES
Effect IT enables direct communication without going through hierarchy; Middle management layers become redundant
Result Flatter organizational structures possible; Fewer hierarchical levels; Wider spans of control
Implication Reduced management overhead; Faster decision-making; Direct accountability
ENABLING GEOGRAPHIC DISTRIBUTION
Remote Work IT enables people to work from anywhere; Offices no longer required for coordination
Global Teams Can assemble teams across continents; Virtual coordination through IT systems
Distributed Org Organization no longer bound by geography; 24/7 operations possible across time zones
ENABLING MATRIX STRUCTURES
Complex Coordination IT systems track dual reporting relationships, resource allocation, project status across matrix
Information Transparency Both functional & project managers can see same real-time data; Reduces conflicts over information
Feasibility Matrix structures become practical at scale because IT handles coordination complexity
ENABLING NETWORK STRUCTURES
Digital Platforms IT creates platforms connecting core firm with partner network; E-commerce, cloud services, APIs
Real-time Integration IT integrates partner systems seamlessly; Supply chain, order processing, quality monitoring
Partnership Governance IT enables monitoring & control of partner performance; Contract enforcement automation
CHANGING POWER DYNAMICS
Information Power IT democratizes information access; Previously, information = power; Now transparent information available to all
Expertise Shift IT expertise becomes critical power source; Technical experts gain influence in organizations
Process Automation IT encodes organizational processes; Reduces discretion of individual managers; Power shift from people to systems
CHALLENGES OF IT-ENABLED STRUCTURES
Implementation Complexity Requires change management; Training; System integration; Can be slow, expensive
Cultural Resistance People resist transparency, surveillance, automation of their roles; Loss of discretion
System Dependency Organization becomes dependent on IT systems; System failures disrupt entire operations
Over-Standardization IT systems enforce standardized processes; Can reduce flexibility, innovation, local adaptation
TECHNOLOGY-STRUCTURE RELATIONSHIP
Enabler, Not Determiner: IT enables structural changes but does not determine them - choice remains with management
Woodward Revisited: Technology impacts structure - IT is fastest-changing technology variable in modern organizations
Future Implication: As IT becomes ubiquitous, network structures & distributed teams will become norm

📊 STRUCTURES SUMMARY & KEY EXAM CONCEPTS
Comparison & Critical Points for Success
STRUCTURES COMPARISON
FUNCTIONAL STRUCTURE
Best For Single product, stable env, cost control critical, functional excellence
Key Advantage Efficiency, cost reduction, expertise depth, career paths
Key Problem Silos, slow response, poor customer focus, coordination overhead
DIVISIONAL (M-FORM)
Best For Diversified firms, multiple products/markets, decentralized strategy
Key Advantage P&L accountability, customer focus, flexibility, general mgmt development
Key Problem Duplication, lost economies of scale, fragmented identity, internal competition
MATRIX STRUCTURE
Best For Dual focus need, complex projects, specialists needed in multiple projects
Key Advantage Combines functional expertise with project coordination, efficient specialist use
Key Problem Unity violation, power struggles, high admin cost, decision paralysis, employee stress
NETWORK/VIRTUAL
Best For Outsourceable functions, high flexibility needed, global operations, gig economy
Key Advantage Flexibility, low costs, global access, rapid scaling, no legacy burden
Key Problem Loss of control, coordination complexity, IP risk, culture issues, accountability diffusion
