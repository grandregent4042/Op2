CONFLICT MANAGEMENT IN ORGANISATIONAL BEHAVIOUR

From Inevitable Tension to Constructive Problem-Solving & Harmony

Evolution of Conflict Views & Types

📈 EVOLUTION OF CONFLICT VIEWS
CONFLICT PERSPECTIVES
Three Evolutionary Perspectives
Traditional View (1930s-1940s)
Conflict is harmful and detrimental
Conflict reflects management failure. Must be eliminated or prevented. Reflects negative organizational climate. Focus on avoiding/suppressing conflict
Human Relations View (1940s-1970s)
Conflict is natural and inevitable
Accepted as normal part of organizational life. Focus shifts to acceptance rather than elimination. Emphasis on understanding and managing rather than preventing
Interactionist View (1970s-Present)
Optimal level of conflict is necessary
Some conflict prevents stagnation, stimulates innovation, fosters creativity. Zero conflict = mediocrity. Seeks to maintain functional conflict levels while minimizing destructive conflict
Modern Understanding
Conflict is neither inherently good nor bad. Depends on type, intensity, and management. Functional conflict drives improvement; dysfunctional conflict damages relationships and performance
⚔️ TYPES OF CONFLICT
Intrapersonal Forms (Internal Conflict)
Approach-Approach Conflict
Two equally attractive goals. Cannot choose both. Example: accept promotion requiring relocation vs stay in current familiar position. Least stressful form
Avoidance-Avoidance Conflict
Two equally unattractive options. Must choose lesser evil. Example: accept unsatisfactory project or face disciplinary action. Highly stressful, often leads to frustration
Approach-Avoidance Conflict
Single goal has both positive and negative aspects. Example: high-paying job but requires extensive travel away from family. Most stressful, creates ambivalence
Conflict by Locus (Levels)
Interpersonal: Between two individuals. Most common in organizations. Personal clashes, role conflicts
Intragroup: Within a team or department. Subgroups, factions, competing interests within group
Intergroup: Between departments or teams. Resource competition, status competition, goal incompatibility
Interorganisational: Between organizations. Supplier-buyer tensions, competitors, stakeholder conflicts
Conflict by Nature
Task Conflict (Functional)
Disagreement about task content and goals. Debate about best approaches, methods, priorities. Can be productive: Different perspectives lead to better solutions, innovation
Relationship Conflict (Dysfunctional)
Personal animosity, interpersonal tensions, disagreements about non-task issues. Almost always destructive: Damages relationships, reduces cooperation, creates hostile environment

Conflict Process & Handling Styles

📊 PONDY'S FIVE-STAGE CONFLICT PROCESS
CONFLICT EVOLUTION
The Five Stages
Stage 1: Latent Conflict
Underlying conditions exist that could produce conflict (competition for resources, role ambiguity, value differences). Not yet conscious or expressed. Hidden beneath surface
Stage 2: Perceived Conflict
Parties become aware of conflict conditions. Recognition that goals are incompatible or interests diverge. Mental awareness but not yet emotional involvement
Stage 3: Felt Conflict
Emotional involvement, anxiety, tension, stress. Personal feelings activated. Conflict becomes personal, not just intellectual. Hormonal and physiological responses begin
Stage 4: Manifest Conflict
Overt conflict behavior expressed. Arguments, fights, passive resistance, sabotage. Open disagreement and confrontation. Visible to others. Most obvious stage
Stage 5: Conflict Aftermath
Aftermath of confrontation. Either resolution or suppression. Can lead to improved relationships (functional outcome) or lingering resentment (dysfunctional). Sets stage for future conflicts
🪟 JOHARI WINDOW & COMMUNICATION CLARITY
Understanding Self-Other Awareness
Open Area (Arena)
Known to self and others. Open communication, transparency. What you know about yourself and others know about you. Enlarging this area reduces conflict
Blind Spot
Known to others but not to self. Behaviors you don't recognize but others see clearly. Feedback from others reveals blind spots. Conflicts often stem from blind spots
Hidden Facade (Concealed)
Known to self but hidden from others. Things you keep private, conceal deliberately. Self-disclosure reduces hidden area. Building trust increases openness
Unknown Area
Unknown to both self and others. Potential, unrealized capabilities, unconscious motivations. Personal growth and therapy expand this area
Conflict Prevention Through Johari Window
Enlarge Open Area through feedback and self-disclosure. Better understanding reduces misinterpretations and conflicts. Feedback reveals blind spots; self-disclosure reduces hidden facades
🛡️ THOMAS-KILMANN HANDLING STYLES
Five Conflict Response Modes
Competing (Assertive, Uncooperative)
Pursue own interests at expense of others. Win-lose mentality. Appropriate when decisions are urgent or unpopular but necessary. Creates resentment if overused
Collaborating (Assertive, Cooperative)
Seek win-win solutions. Both parties' interests are important. Take time, open communication, creative problem-solving. Best for important conflicts. Strongest relationships result
Compromising (Moderate)
Both parties give up something. Middle ground solution. Faster than collaboration but less satisfying. Good when time is limited and stakes are moderate
Accommodating (Unassertive, Cooperative)
Put other's interests before own. Yield to maintain relationships. Good for minor issues or when relationship is more important than issue. Can build goodwill
Avoiding (Unassertive, Uncooperative)
Ignore or postpone conflict. Sidestep the issue. Appropriate for trivial matters or cooling-off periods. Overuse lets problems fester and grow
Situational Appropriateness
No single best style. Context determines effectiveness. Effective managers use all five styles situationally. Collaboration for important issues; competing for urgent decisions; avoiding for trivial matters

Role Conflict & Management Strategies

👤 ROLE CONFLICT & STRESS
ROLE DYNAMICS
Types of Role Conflict
Inter-Role Conflict
Competing demands from multiple roles. Work vs family roles. Manager vs team member. Professional vs personal identity. Example: lead team meeting vs attend child's event simultaneously
Intra-Role Conflict
Conflicting expectations within same role. Boss wants quick turnaround, quality standards require thorough work. Wants you to be friendly and authoritative simultaneously
Person-Role Conflict
Role expectations clash with personal values/beliefs. Ethical job requirements vs moral principles. "Selling" product you don't believe in. Requires compromising integrity
Role Overload
Too many responsibilities, not enough time/resources. Impossible to meet all expectations. Leads to stress, burnout, quality decline. Common in restructured organizations
Role Ambiguity
Unclear expectations about what role entails. Uncertain about responsibilities, authority, performance criteria. Anxiety and stress from not knowing what's expected
Solutions for Role Conflict
Role Clarification
Clear job descriptions, explicit expectations, regular discussions with manager. Remove ambiguity. Align role with capabilities. Everyone understands requirements
Priority Rules
Establish clear hierarchy of competing demands. What takes precedence? Which expectations are non-negotiable? Provides decision framework when conflicts arise
Structural Redesign
Reorganize roles and responsibilities to reduce conflicts. Eliminate overlapping duties, clarify boundaries between positions. Reduce workload or add resources
🔧 CONFLICT MANAGEMENT STRATEGIES
Structural Approaches
Superordinate Goals
Create shared objectives that transcend conflict. "We win together or lose together." Common enemy effect. Focus on larger purpose binding parties together
Resource Augmentation
Provide more resources so both parties can achieve goals. Removes zero-sum competition. If competing for budget, increase budget. Eliminates scarcity-based conflict
Appeals to Hierarchy
Higher authority makes binding decision. Quick resolution but may create resentment. Losers may not accept decision. Use when other methods fail
Behavioral & Interpersonal Approaches
Transactional Analysis
Analyze communication patterns (Parent, Adult, Child ego states). Switch to Adult-to-Adult communication. Understand hidden scripts and games. Improve dialogue quality
Mediation & Arbitration
Mediation: Neutral third party facilitates resolution, parties decide
Arbitration: Arbitrator makes binding decision. Mediation preferred for relationships
Integrative Bargaining
Expand the pie rather than fight over existing pie. Find creative solutions where both gain. Understand underlying interests, not just stated positions. Win-win orientation

Indian Context & Contemporary Challenges

🇮🇳 CONFLICT IN INDIAN ORGANISATIONS
INDIAN CONTEXT
Cultural & Structural Factors
High Power Distance Culture
Hierarchical acceptance of unequal distribution of power. Employees hesitant to challenge authority or voice disagreement with superiors. Conflict suppression rather than resolution. Resentment builds internally
Industrial Relations Code 2020
India's new legal framework for employment relations. Emphasis on structured grievance redressal, mandatory conciliation, worker participation, dispute resolution mechanisms. Aims to formalize conflict management
Caste & Community Tensions
Caste-based discrimination in Indian workplaces despite legal prohibition. Community/regional identity conflicts. Religious tensions. Conflicts rooted in social structure, not just task/role issues. Extremely sensitive
Multi-Generational Gaps
Growing workforce diversity: Baby Boomers, Gen X, Millennials, Gen Z. Different work values, communication styles, authority respect levels. Generational conflicts increasing. Older generation values loyalty/hierarchy; younger values flexibility/autonomy
Key Implications for Indian Managers
Power Distance: Can't use egalitarian/collaborative approaches directly. Need to respect hierarchy while creating safe spaces for feedback. One-on-one meetings better than group forums
Legal Compliance: Must follow Industrial Relations Code procedures. Formal documentation, arbitration, worker representation essential. Informal conflict resolution no longer sufficient
Identity Sensitivity: Caste, religion, region are sensitive issues. Need explicit anti-discrimination policies. Diversity training mandatory. Create inclusive culture, not just compliance
Generational Management: Mix of traditional and modern approaches. Mentor younger by older employees. Create mentorship programs. Acknowledge different working styles and values
⚡ CONTEMPORARY CHALLENGES
Emerging Conflict Sources
Remote & Hybrid Work Conflicts
Lack of face-to-face interaction increases misunderstandings. Asynchronous communication breeds conflict. Time zone conflicts. Isolation creates tension. Less informal resolution opportunities
Algorithmic Management
AI-based performance tracking, automated discipline systems create perceptions of unfairness. Employees feel lack of human judgment. "Fighting the system" becomes new conflict type
Work Intensification & Burnout
High stress, impossible deadlines breed interpersonal conflicts. Exhausted employees have lower patience, more irritability. Relationships suffer under pressure. Resource competition increases
Psychological Safety & Voice
Despite talk of psychological safety, many fear consequences of speaking up. Silent conflicts build. Unresolved tensions manifest as disengagement or sabotage. Need to build genuine safe environments
✅ BEST PRACTICES FOR EFFECTIVE CONFLICT MANAGEMENT
Integrated Approach
Combine structural, behavioral, and legal approaches. Clarify roles and goals (structure), develop emotional intelligence and communication skills (behavior), follow proper procedures (legal). Address conflicts early before they escalate
Key Success Factors
Early Intervention: Address conflicts in latent or perceived stages. Prevention better than cure
Psychological Safety: Create environment where people safely express concerns without fear
Collaborative Problem-Solving: Focus on shared interests, not positions. Seek win-win solutions
Follow-Up: After resolution, monitor and ensure agreements are honored. Rebuild relationships
