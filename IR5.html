ILO
KEY FACTS AT A GLANCE (Exam Quick Reference)
Founded 1919 — Part XIII of Treaty of Versailles; post-WWI peace settlement
UN Specialised Agency 1946 — FIRST UN specialised agency; ONLY tripartite UN body
Nobel Peace Prize 1969 — for improving fraternity among nations & pursuing social justice
Headquarters Geneva, Switzerland
Members 187 member states (2025) — near-universal membership
Total Conventions & Recommendations 190 Conventions + 206 Recommendations adopted (2025)
India's Relationship FOUNDING MEMBER (1919) — one of 29 originals; PERMANENT Governing Body seat; 47/190 Conventions ratified (2025)
4-PILLAR FOUNDING RATIONALE (Critical Exam Concept)
1. Social Justice Pillar "Labour conditions involving injustice, hardship and privation produce industrial unrest that endangers world peace."
2. Economic Pillar Non-improvement of labour conditions in any country obstructs other nations' efforts — 'race to the bottom' in labour standards must be prevented
3. Humanitarian Pillar Workers are human beings, not commodities; employment involves dignity and rights, not merely transactions
4. Political Pillar Rising socialist movements post-WWI demanded institutional response to worker grievances — ILO as democratic alternative to revolution
ILO PREAMBLE QUOTE (For Exam Opening)
MUST-QUOTE PREAMBLE "Universal and lasting peace can be established only if it is based upon social justice." — ILO Constitution Preamble, 1919
Why Quote This? Opens any ILO answer elegantly — establishes founding rationale in one line; shows understanding of ILO's core mission
TRIPARTISM - ILO's DEFINING FEATURE (CRITICAL)
What is Tripartism? ILO is the ONLY international organisation where non-governmental entities (employer and worker organisations) have EQUAL STANDING with governments in ALL deliberations
Significance Conventions adopted at ILC require consensus across government, employer, and worker delegates — far more demanding legitimacy test than any intergovernmental treaty
Comparative Advantage No other UN body (WHO, UNESCO, etc.) has this tripartite structure — makes ILO unique in global governance

🏛️ ILO STRUCTURE - Three Principal Organs
International Labour Conference | Governing Body | International Labour Office | Composition & Functions
3 ORGANS
ORGAN 1: INTERNATIONAL LABOUR CONFERENCE (ILC)
Called (Exam Name) "Parliament of Labour" — unique naming reflects its legislative role
Composition (CRITICAL — Exam Frequent) 2 government + 1 employer + 1 worker representative per member state; EACH delegate votes INDEPENDENTLY — NOT as one national bloc
Frequency ANNUAL — held in Geneva (June)
Functions (5 Key Functions) (i) Sets international labour standards — adopts Conventions & Recommendations (ii) Supreme policy-making body (iii) Debates global labour issues (iv) Approves ILO's budget (v) Elects Governing Body
Unique Feature (EXAM CRITICAL) Employer and worker reps can vote AGAINST their own government's position — demonstrates ILO's independence from national politics
ORGAN 2: GOVERNING BODY (GB)
Composition 56 members — 28 government + 14 employer + 14 worker members
10 PERMANENT SEATS (INDIA KEY — PYQ FREQUENT) 10 seats for states of 'chief industrial importance': USA, UK, France, Germany, Japan, China, Brazil, Russia, INDIA, Italy — India's permanent seat is HIGH EXAM VALUE
Frequency 3 times per year — March, June, October
Functions (5 Key Functions) (i) Executive council of ILO (ii) Decides ILO policy, programme, budget (iii) Appoints Director-General (iv) Convenes ILC (v) Sets ILC agenda
India's Influence PERMANENT seat means India participates in executive decisions; influences ILO's global labour agenda directly
ORGAN 3: INTERNATIONAL LABOUR OFFICE
What It Is NOT the ILO itself — it is ILO's permanent secretariat and operational arm
Composition & Location Director-General + ~3,000 permanent staff from 150+ countries; multidisciplinary (economists, lawyers, social scientists); Geneva, Switzerland
Functions (5 Key Functions) (i) Permanent secretariat & operational arm (ii) Prepares documentation for ILC & Governing Body (iii) Provides technical assistance to member states (iv) Conducts research (v) Publishes WESO, World of Work Reports, ILOSTAT
Budget ~$850 million biennially
Key Products (EXAM IMPORTANT) WESO (World Employment and Social Outlook) | World of Work Reports | Global Wage Report | ILOSTAT (world's largest labour statistics database)
CONVENTIONS vs RECOMMENDATIONS (Critical Distinction)
Convention International treaty; 2/3 majority at ILC; sent to states for ratification. YES — once ratified by state, becomes national obligation (e.g. Convention 87, 98)
Recommendation Guidelines; supplements Conventions or stands alone; 2/3 majority at ILC. NO — non-binding; guides national policy & legislation (e.g. Recommendation 198)
Protocol Amendment/extension of existing Convention; requires ratification like a Convention. YES — once ratified (e.g. Protocol of 2014 to Convention 29)
1998 ILO Declaration (CRITICAL) Identified 8 Conventions as 'Core/Fundamental' — BINDING on ALL members regardless of ratification, as expressions of ILO membership obligations

CORE & FUNCTIONS
8 CORE CONVENTIONS (1998 Declaration)
1. Convention 29 (1930) — Forced Labour Prohibits forced/compulsory labour in ALL forms. India: ✓ RATIFIED — Bonded Labour System Act 1976; Art. 23 Constitution
2. Convention 87 (1948) — Freedom of Association Right of workers AND employers to form/join organisations freely. India: ✗ NOT RATIFIED — public servants restricted; essential services cited
3. Convention 98 (1949) — Right to Organise & CB Protection against anti-union discrimination; promotes CB. India: ✗ NOT RATIFIED — no compulsory recognition law nationally
4. Convention 100 (1951) — Equal Remuneration Equal pay for work of equal value — no gender wage discrimination. India: ✓ RATIFIED — Equal Remuneration Act 1976 (now Code on Wages 2019)
5. Convention 105 (1957) — Abolition of Forced Labour Abolishes forced labour as political coercion/punishment. India: ✓ RATIFIED
6. Convention 111 (1958) — Discrimination (Employment) No discrimination (race, colour, sex, religion, political opinion, national origin). India: ✓ RATIFIED — Equal Remuneration Act; SC/ST protections
7. Convention 138 (1973) — Minimum Age Sets minimum age for employment — eliminates child labour. India: ✓ RATIFIED — Child Labour Act 1986/2016; RTE Act 2009
8. Convention 182 (1999) — Worst Forms of Child Labour Immediate elimination: trafficking, debt bondage, prostitution, hazardous work. India: ✓ RATIFIED
INDIA'S RATIFICATION GAP (CRITICAL EXAM POINT)
India's Status: 47/190 Conventions (2025) RATIFIED 5 of 8 CORE Conventions; NOT ratified 87, 98, others
MOST SIGNIFICANT NON-RATIFICATION Convention 87 (Freedom of Association) & Convention 98 (Right to Organise & CB)
Government Argument Indian law (Art. 19(1)(c)) already protects; essential services restrictions; public sector concerns
CRITICAL ISSUE Non-ratification of 87 & 98 undermines India's credibility as champion of workers' rights in ILO's own ILC
ILO FUNCTIONS - STAR FRAMEWORK (6 Functions)
S — Standard-Setting Adoption of Conventions & Recommendations — primary function. 190 Conventions + 206 Recommendations (2025)
T — Technical Assistance & Capacity Building Advisory services, training, policy guidance to member states. DECLARE Programme (child labour); IPEC (helped India reduce child labour 65% 2001–2011)
A — Applied Research & Knowledge Production WESO (World Employment and Social Outlook); Global Wage Report; ILOSTAT — world's largest labour statistics database
R — Rights At Work & Social Dialogue Tripartite consultation at national/international levels. Convention 144 mandates national tripartite consultation. Indian Labour Conference inspired by ILC
Decent Work Agenda (1999) Overarching strategic framework — 4 pillars (Employment, Social Protection, Social Dialogue, Rights). Linked to SDG 8
Humanitarian/Crisis Response Labour market analysis in post-conflict/disaster situations. COVID-19 WESO series; Gulf migrant worker protections
DECENT WORK AGENDA (1999) — 4 PILLARS (PYQ FREQUENT)
PILLAR 1: EMPLOYMENT Full, productive, freely chosen employment for all; quality jobs; sustainable enterprises
PILLAR 2: SOCIAL PROTECTION Social security; workplace safety; income support; protection against unemployment, sickness, old age
PILLAR 3: SOCIAL DIALOGUE Tripartism; CB; freedom of association; right to organise and negotiate
PILLAR 4: RIGHTS AT WORK Fundamental rights; no forced labour; no child labour; no discrimination; freedom of association
SDG 8 Link (EXAM CURRENT AFFAIRS) ILO leads global monitoring of Decent Work targets; India's SDG 8 reporting depends on ILO benchmarks

INDIA IMPACT
INDIA'S RELATIONSHIP WITH ILO (Founding Member Context)
Founding Membership (1919) One of 29 original founding members — colonial anomaly: India under British rule but represented independently at Versailles due to WWI contribution
Permanent Governing Body Seat One of 10 permanent seats as 'state of chief industrial importance' — strategic influence over ILO agenda
Ratification Record 47/190 Conventions ratified (2025) — selective but significant; non-ratification of 87 & 98 remains critical gap
Technical Cooperation ILO has had CONTINUOUS Technical Cooperation programmes in India since 1950 — LARGEST country programme in South Asia
G20 Presidency (2023) India's G20 Labour & Employment Working Group advanced Decent Work Agenda & future of work discussions — strengthened ILO-India alignment
ILO INFLUENCE ON INDIAN LABOUR LAW (Convention-to-Act Mapping)
Conv. 29 — Forced Labour (1930) → Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act 1976; Criminal Law Amendment; Art. 23 Constitution
Conv. 100 — Equal Remuneration (1951) → Equal Remuneration Act 1976 → Code on Wages 2019
Conv. 111 — Non-Discrimination (1958) → SC/ST protections; gender equality in employment; Art. 15, 16 Constitution
Conv. 138 & 182 — Child Labour → Child Labour Act 1986/2016 Amendment; RTE Act 2009
Conv. 131 — Minimum Wage (1970) → Minimum Wages Act 1948 → Code on Wages 2019; National Floor Level MW concept
Conv. 144 — Tripartite Consultation → Indian Labour Conference; Standing Labour Committee; CIEC (Confederated Indian Employers' Council)
Decent Work Agenda (1999) → National Policy on Safety, Health & Environment 2009; 4 Labour Codes consolidation
ILO'S INFLUENCE ON INDIA'S IR SYSTEM & TU MOVEMENT
Tripartite Model (ILC → ILC/SLC in India) ILC's tripartite structure inspired India's Indian Labour Conference — first ILC held 1942; apex tripartite body now. Standing Labour Committee continues tradition
Recognition Rights (Conv. 87 & 98 — NOT Ratified) ILO's Conventions 87 & 98 (unratified) influenced academic & legal discourse on compulsory union recognition; still unresolved in India
International Solidarity Indian unions affiliated with international TU confederations linked to ILO — INTUC with ITUC; CITU with WFTU; connects to global labour movement
Labour Standards in Trade ILO standards increasingly linked to trade agreements — pressure on Indian exporters (garments, leather) to comply with core labour standards
IPEC & CHILD LABOUR SUCCESS STORY (India-Specific)
IPEC Programme (International Programme on Elimination of Child Labour) Direct cooperation between ILO & India on child labour elimination — India LARGEST IPEC partner in Asia
Results (2001–2023) India reduced child labour from 12.6 million (2001) to ~3 million (2023) — ILO credited IPEC partnership & legislative reform (Child Labour Act 2016)
Remaining Challenges Child domestic workers, agricultural labour in India still significant — ILO recommendations for targeted interventions
NCLP (National Child Labour Project) Joint IPEC-funded projects; rehabilitation schemes; vocational training for withdrawn children

CRITICAL EVALUATION: IS ILO STILL RELEVANT? (PYQ 2016, 20M)
✓ ARGUMENTS FOR Continued Relevance (5) DWA alignment with India's goals | Gig economy guidance (2019 Report) | Migrant worker protection (18M Indian workers) | SDG 8 monitoring | Labour Code reform drew on ILO recommendations
✗ ARGUMENTS AGAINST / LIMITATIONS (5) No enforcement mechanism (purely persuasive) | Selective Indian ratification (avoids sensitive issues) | North-South divide (standards seen as protectionist) | Implementation gap (ratified Conventions poorly enforced) | Informal economy gap (~90% of India's workforce outside practical scope)
BALANCED EXAM CONCLUSION — 'ILO IS STILL RELEVANT' ILO's relevance is NOT diminished by lack of enforcement powers. Its influence operates through NORM DIFFUSION, TECHNICAL COOPERATION, and STANDARD-SETTING — mechanisms that demonstrably shaped Indian labour law for 100+ years. Challenge: not ILO's relevance but India's selective engagement. Widen ratification, strengthen implementation of ratified Conventions, align Labour Codes with ILO's DWA
STRENGTHS OF ILO (4 Key Strengths)
1. Tripartism Uniqueness ONLY international organisation where employers & workers have equal standing with governments
2. Norm Diffusion Power Conventions create moral suasion & peer pressure; demonstrated through 100+ years of influence on Indian labour law
3. Technical Cooperation Capacity Direct assistance to developing nations (IPEC programme exemplar); capacity-building on labour issues
4. Knowledge Production Authority WESO, ILOSTAT, Global Wage Report — world-respected labour data & analysis inform global policymaking
WEAKNESSES / LIMITATIONS (4 Key Limitations)
1. No Enforcement Mechanism Cannot compel ratification or compliance; purely persuasive; relies on soft power
2. Geopolitical Tensions China-India-West dynamics contest ILO governance & standard neutrality; harder to politicise than bilateral/trade bodies but contested
3. Informal Economy Gap ~90% of India's workforce outside practical scope of ILO standards designed for formal employment
4. Implementation Deficit in Ratified Countries Even ratified Conventions have wide law-practice gap (child labour, forced labour); limited accountability for non-compliance

✈️ MIGRANT WORKERS & DOMESTIC WORKERS - ILO Standards & India Fulfillment
Convention 97, 143, 189 | Multilateral Framework | Fair Recruitment | Extent of Fulfillment in India
VULNERABLE WORKERS
ILO STANDARDS FOR MIGRANT WORKERS (3 Conventions)
Conv. 97 & 143 — Migrant Workers Equal treatment; regulation of recruitment agencies; equal access to employment & social benefits. India: ✗ NOT RATIFIED
Multilateral Framework on Labour Migration (2006) Non-binding; rights-based approach to labour migration governance. India's Emigration Act 1983 reform discussions use this framework
Recommendation 86 — Migration for Employment Bilateral agreements for migrant worker protection; information before departure. India has bilateral MoUs with several Gulf countries
Fair Recruitment Principles (2016) No fees charged to workers by recruiters; contracts honoured; information transparency. India: recruitment agent regulation under Emigration Act; e-MIGRATE portal
INDIA'S MIGRANT WORKER CONTEXT (18+ Million Workers)
Scale of Migration 18+ million Indian migrants in Gulf; primarily construction, hospitality, domestic work; major source of remittances (>$80 billion/year)
Non-Ratification of Conv. 97, 143 India NOT RATIFIED; government uses BILATERAL MoUs instead with major receiving countries (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar)
Enforcement Challenges MOIA (Ministry of Overseas Indian Affairs) limited capacity; enforcement of recruitment agent regulations weak; worker awareness of rights minimal
E-MIGRATE Portal Online registration & documentation system; transparency on recruitment; not yet achieving full ILO Fair Recruitment Principles compliance
DOMESTIC WORKERS — MAJOR PROTECTION GAP
Conv. 189 (2011) — Domestic Workers Convention Decent work for domestic workers; minimum wage, rest periods, social protection. India: ✗ NOT RATIFIED — CRITICAL GAP
India's Domestic Workers (Current Status) Estimated 4.75 million domestic workers in India WITHOUT comprehensive legal protection
Legal Vacuum Domestic Worker Protection Bill drafted but NOT passed; no comprehensive domestic worker law; excluded from many labour laws
Policy Development National Domestic Workers Policy under consideration by Ministry of Labour; ILO advocacy continuous since 2011
EXAM TIP — India's Reluctance India reluctant to ratify Conv. 189 due to domestic political resistance (informal sector, employment in homes, private sphere concerns)
EXTENT OF FULFILLMENT IN INDIA — TABLE (6 Areas)
Child Labour: Largely Fulfilled Conv. 182 ratified; Child Labour Act 2016 strengthens prohibition; BUT implementation in agriculture, informal sector weak; hazardous child labour still prevalent
Equal Remuneration: Partially Fulfilled Conv. 100 ratified; Code on Wages 2019 implemented; BUT gender pay gap persists (~30% gap per ILO data); enforcement in informal sector weak
Freedom of Association: Partially Fulfilled Constitutional protection (Art. 19(1)(c)); BUT Conv. 87, 98 NOT ratified; no compulsory recognition law; public sector restrictions
Migrant Worker Protection: Poorly Fulfilled Bilateral MoUs with major receiving countries; BUT Conv. 97 & 143 not ratified; enforcement abroad weak; MOIA capacity limited
Social Protection: Partially Fulfilled Code on Social Security 2020 extends to gig workers; BUT implementation rules incomplete (2025); ~50% of formal workers lack social security coverage
Domestic Workers: NOT Fulfilled Conv. 189 not ratified; no comprehensive domestic worker law; 4.75 million workers entirely unprotected
