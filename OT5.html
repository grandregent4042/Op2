🎨 MEANING & CONCEPT OF CULTURE
Core Definitions & Strategic Functions
ORG CULTURE
CORE DEFINITIONS
Definition 1 "Set of assumptions, beliefs, values and norms shared by organization's members"
Definition 2 "Established customs and norms that specify how people behave within an organization"
Definition 3 "System of shared meaning that guides behavior and provides organizational identity"
Key Point QUOTE DIRECTLY in answers - definitions are frequently asked in exams
6 STRATEGIC FUNCTIONS
1. Identity Provides sense of organizational identity and distinctiveness
2. Commitment Facilitates commitment to organization beyond economic interests
3. Social System Stability Provides stability, continuity, and coherence to social system
4. Sense-Making Helps members interpret events and understand organizational reality
5. Behavior Guidance Guides and shapes behavior through informal norms and expectations
6. Performance Strong culture correlates with organizational performance and effectiveness
WHY CULTURE MATTERS
Long-term Survival Culture is long-term phenomenon that decides success/failure and org survival
Competitive Advantage Culture creates competitive advantage difficult for competitors to replicate
Change Management Understanding culture critical for managing organizational change successfully
Performance Link Research shows strong culture correlates with higher performance, lower turnover
EXAM TIP: QUOTE DEFINITIONS DIRECTLY
Remember: Definitions of organizational culture are frequently quoted directly in exam answers - memorize at least 2-3 definitions for direct use

📊 SCHEIN'S THREE-LEVEL MODEL - THE ESSENTIAL FRAMEWORK
Artifacts, Espoused Values, Basic Assumptions
SCHEIN 3-LEVEL
LEVEL 1: ARTIFACTS
Definition Visible, observable physical environment, behavior, language, rituals, dress code, stories, symbols
Examples Office layout, uniforms, company jargon, ceremonies, awards, organizational symbols, myths & legends
Characteristic Easy to observe; Difficult to decipher meaning; Multiple interpretations possible; Most visible level
Note "Artifacts" is plural - always refer to cultural artifacts as multiple observable elements
LEVEL 2: ESPOUSED VALUES
Definition Stated goals, philosophies, ideologies - what organization claims to stand for (official culture)
Examples Mission statements, code of ethics, corporate values, official policy statements, published philosophy
Gap from Reality Often differs from actual practice - what org says it believes vs what it actually does (espoused vs enacted)
Problem Can be aspirational or gap-filling - members may not truly believe in espoused values
LEVEL 3: BASIC ASSUMPTIONS
Definition Unconscious, taken-for-granted beliefs about reality; deepest level of culture
Characteristics UNCONSCIOUS - members don't realize they hold them; SELF-SEALING - seem immutable and indisputable
Example "People are inherently lazy and need to be monitored" - deeply held but invisible assumption
Impact Most powerful driver of behavior; Hardest to change because unconscious; Influence both levels above
INTERCONNECTIONS
Hierarchy Basic assumptions → Espoused values → Artifacts (underlying → public → observable)
Visibility Inverse Artifacts most visible, assumptions least visible; Difficulty increases as you go deeper
Congruence Strong cultures have alignment across all 3 levels; Weak cultures have gaps between levels
WHY THIS FRAMEWORK IS ESSENTIAL
Explains Culture Depth: Shows culture is not just what's observable - has deep unconscious elements
Explains Difficulty of Change: Hard to change because basic assumptions are unconscious and self-sealing
Explains Gap: Why organizations' stated values often differ from actual practice

🔨 HOW ORGANISATIONAL CULTURE FORMS
7-Stage Process (PYQ 2021 - 20M)
CULTURE FORMS
7-STAGE FORMATION
Stage 1: Founder's Vision Founder establishes core values, assumptions, and cultural direction based on personal beliefs and experiences
Stage 2: Initial Culture Embedding Founder hires people who share values; Establishes initial norms, practices, and rewards
Stage 3: Crisis & Testing Crises test whether cultural values are really held; How org responds shapes culture
Stage 4: Reinforcement Successful responses reinforced; Stories, legends, heroes emerge; Culture crystallizes
Stage 5: Stabilization Culture becomes stable; New members socialized into existing culture; Assumptions deepen
Stage 6: Tradition Culture becomes traditional; "We do it this way because that's how we've always done it"
Stage 7: Self-Perpetuation Culture self-perpetuating; Unconscious basic assumptions guide all behavior
CRITICAL INCIDENTS
Definition Specific events that dramatically shape organizational culture based on organizational response
Types of Incidents Crisis (financial, scandal), Leadership change, Major decision under pressure, Success/failure with significant impact
Culture Impact How org responds to critical incidents shapes what members believe org really values vs espoused values
Example If org says "quality first" but cuts quality to save costs during downturn - reveals true values through crisis response
KEY MECHANISMS
Founder Influence Initial culture shaped entirely by founder's vision, values, personality; founder sets foundation
Hiring Decisions Each hiring reinforces culture by selecting people who fit; Shapes cultural continuity
Stories & Heroes Stories of critical incidents become organizational legends; Heroes embody cultural values
Responses to Crisis How organization responds to challenges reveals and shapes true culture; Tests commitment to values
PYQ 2021 (20M) INSIGHT
Question Focus: How culture forms through 7-stage process and role of critical incidents in shaping culture
Answer Strategy: Explain stages, emphasize founder role, discuss critical incidents as culture-shaping events, give examples

🔐 SUSTAINING ORGANISATIONAL CULTURE - ROBBINS' FRAMEWORK
Selection, Top Management, Socialisation
SUSTAIN CULTURE
1. SELECTION MECHANISM
Process Hiring process filters for cultural fit - even when formal criteria seem objective, interviewers make cultural judgments
How It Works Recruiters unconsciously ask questions, observe behaviors, look for "fit" with organizational culture
Sustaining Effect Continuously hires people similar to existing culture; Perpetuates existing culture forward
Problem Excessive selection for fit creates HOMOGENEITY and reduces COGNITIVE DIVERSITY - systematically excludes diverse candidates
2. TOP MANAGEMENT BEHAVIOR
Most Powerful Mechanism Leaders are culture's MOST POWERFUL EMBODIMENT of what org values
What Communicates What leaders pay attention to, measure, react to, and model communicates cultural priorities FAR MORE POWERFULLY than formal statements
Walk the Talk "Walk the talk" is NOT OPTIONAL - it is the PRIMARY culture-sustaining mechanism
Role Modeling Leaders' visible behavior sets tone; Employees watch what leaders do, not just what they say
3. SOCIALISATION MECHANISM
Pre-Arrival Phase Prospective members join with prior assumptions and expectations; Before joining organization
Encounter Phase Reality shock - confronting the actual organizational culture (often different from expectations)
Metamorphosis Phase CRITICAL PHASE - adaptation and achievement of cultural belonging; Member internalizes culture
Outcome Orgs that socialize well have LOWER EARLY TURNOVER; Members who successfully metamorphose stay longer
ADDITIONAL MECHANISMS
Stories & Legends Organizational stories about heroes, founders, critical incidents communicate culture
Rituals & Ceremonies Regular practices reinforce cultural values (awards, celebrations, traditions)
Reward Systems What org rewards shapes behavior; Rewards aligned with culture sustain it forward
Physical Environment Office design, dress codes, symbols communicate and sustain culture
CRITICAL INSIGHT: METAMORPHOSIS PHASE
Why It Matters: This is the critical phase where new member actually BECOMES part of culture - not before
Org Responsibility: Orgs with good socialization processes have much lower early turnover - means metamorphosis was successful

⚖️ POSITIVE & NEGATIVE IMPACTS OF CULTURE
The Good & The Dark Side (PYQ 2022 - 20M)
CULTURE IMPACTS
POSITIVE IMPACTS
Coordination & Control Norms provide informal control; Coordinates behavior without explicit rules
Commitment & Motivation Strong culture builds employee commitment beyond economic interests; Intrinsic motivation
Identity & Belonging Provides identity, sense of belonging, psychological connection to organization
Performance Strong culture correlates with higher productivity, lower turnover, better performance
Attraction Strong culture attracts aligned employees; Self-selection mechanism
Competitive Advantage Culture difficult to imitate; Creates sustainable competitive advantage
NEGATIVE IMPACTS
Resistance to Change Strong culture resists necessary change; "We do it this way" becomes barrier to innovation
Homogeneity Excessive selection for fit reduces diversity; Excludes different perspectives systematically
Conformity Pressure Intense pressure to conform; Suppresses dissent, creativity, unconventional thinking
Insularity Strong culture can become insular; Ignores external feedback, market signals
Exclusion May exclude people who don't fit mold; Discriminatory effect against minorities
Obsolescence Culture embedded in past success may become obsolete as environment changes
TRADE-OFFS
Stability vs Adaptability Strong culture provides stability but reduces adaptability to change
Control vs Autonomy Culture provides informal control but may limit individual autonomy & creativity
Fit vs Diversity Selection for cultural fit builds coherence but reduces cognitive diversity
Commitment vs Freedom Strong culture builds commitment but may suppress personal freedom and dissent
PYQ 2022 (20M) ANSWER STRUCTURE
Balanced Approach: Discuss both positive AND negative impacts - strong culture is not universally "good"
Context Matters: Positive impacts in stable environments; Negative impacts in dynamic/changing environments
Trade-offs: Acknowledge inherent tensions in culture - cannot maximize all benefits simultaneously

🌡️ CULTURE vs CLIMATE & CULTURAL TYPOLOGIES
Distinction, Handy, Cameron & Quinn (PYQ 2021)
CULTURE vs CLIMATE
CULTURE vs CLIMATE
Culture: Definition Deep, stable system of shared meaning; Shared assumptions, beliefs, values, norms
Culture: Timeframe Long-term, deeply embedded; Takes years to develop and change
Climate: Definition Surface-level perceptions of organizational environment; Reflects culture but not same as culture
Climate: Timeframe Temporary, changeable; Can shift with leadership change or organizational events
Key Difference Culture is values-based (what we believe); Climate is perception-based (how we feel about it)
HANDY'S 4 ARCHETYPES (1993)
Power Culture Centralized authority; Power at core; Fast decisions; Depends on key leader; Entrepreneurial firms
Role Culture Bureaucratic, rule-based; Position more important than person; Hierarchical; Traditional orgs
Task Culture Project/team oriented; Focus on results; Flexible; Problem-solving focused; Matrix structures
Person Culture Individual centered; Unusual in formal orgs; Found in partnerships, professional firms; Individual autonomy
CAMERON & QUINN FRAMEWORK
Clan Culture Family-like, collaborative, people-oriented, loyalty emphasized, mentoring valued
Adhocracy Culture Entrepreneurial, innovative, risk-taking, dynamic, individual initiative encouraged
Market Culture Results-driven, competitive, customer-focused, performance measured, profit-oriented
Hierarchy Culture Process-driven, rule-based, structured, stability emphasized, efficiency valued
PYQ 2021 - CULTURE vs CLIMATE DISTINCTION
Remember: Culture is DEEP VALUES (what organization fundamentally believes); Climate is SURFACE PERCEPTIONS (how people experience it)
Practical: Climate can change quickly with leadership change; Culture takes years to shift because it's values-based

🔄 CHANGING CULTURE & CULTURAL PROBLEMS
When, How, & Why It's Hard
CHANGE CULTURE
CONDITIONS REQUIRING CHANGE
Dramatic Crisis Financial failure, major scandal, regulatory action, existential threat to organization
Leadership Succession New leadership with different vision often triggers culture change opportunity
Lifecycle Stage Change Startup → Scale → Large org requires different culture; Existing culture becomes barrier
Merger & Integration Merging organizations requires integrating different cultures; Cultural clash common cause of M&A failure
CHANGE STRATEGIES
1. Vision & Need Articulate compelling new vision; Create urgency and need for change
2. Leadership Change Replace/transform key leaders who embody old culture; Symbolic signal of change
3. Restructure Systems Change formal systems, rewards, and reporting to reinforce new values
4. Role Modeling Model new behavior from top - visible walk-the-talk is essential
5. Socialization Socialize new members into new culture; Phase out old culture members gradually
6. Symbols & Stories Create new stories, rituals, and symbols embodying new culture
WHY CULTURE CHANGE IS HARD
Level 3 Unconscious Basic assumptions are UNCONSCIOUS - members don't realize they hold them
Self-Sealing Basic assumptions are SELF-SEALING - seem immutable, indisputable, unchangeable
Cannot Change What You Cannot See Cannot change unconscious assumptions - first must make them visible & articulate them
Identity Threat Culture change threatens organizational identity; Members resist losing familiar culture
Minimum Timeframe Culture change minimum: 3-7 YEARS for meaningful shift; Typically takes longer
CRITICAL INSIGHT: WHY CULTURE CHANGE IS DIFFICULT
Core Issue: Level 3 basic assumptions are UNCONSCIOUS and SELF-SEALING - members don't see them, can't articulate them, can't change what's invisible
Timeframe Reality: Expect 3-7 YEARS minimum for meaningful culture shift - quick culture change is myth
Strategy Implication: First step is SURFACING assumptions - making unconscious conscious through dialogue, crisis, leadership modeling
