Nature & Concepts
📚 Core Definitions
Classic Definitions
Barnard: "Conscious Coordination" — Organizations are systems of consciously coordinated activities designed to accomplish collective goals
Robbins: "Social Unit" — A social entity deliberately structured and coordinated through a system of integrated relationships to achieve goals
Drucker: "Means to Performance" — Organizations exist to get things done that cannot be accomplished by individuals alone
Perspectives on Organisations
Western
"Rational-Legal Authority" — Authority flows from positions and rules; bureaucratic, merit-based
🇮🇳 Indian
"Promoter-Family Authority" — Authority tied to founder/family; hierarchical with personal loyalty
🇮🇳 Indian Context: Many PSUs and family-owned businesses operate under hybrid models blending traditional hierarchies with modern structures. Understanding both perspectives essential for Indian managers.

🌍 Environmental Frameworks
Emery & Trist: Environmental Textures
Dimensions of Environment
Placid-Randomized: Stable, random changes
Placid-Clustered: Stable, but threats cluster together
Disturbed-Reactive: Dynamic, competitors react to moves
Turbulent: Highly dynamic, complex interdependencies
Duncan's Uncertainty Matrix
Simple-Stable
Few factors, low change. Functional structures suit best.
Simple-Dynamic
Few factors, high change. Adaptive mechanisms needed.
Complex-Stable
Many factors, low change. Divisional structures fit.
Complex-Dynamic
Many factors, high change. Matrix/network structures required.
Contingency Principle: Structure-Environment Fit
Core Concept: Organizational effectiveness depends on alignment between structure and environmental characteristics. Different environments require different designs.

Organisational Goals
🎯 Goal Typology
Official vs. Operative Goals
Official Goals
Stated in charters, mission statements. What org says it does publicly.
Operative Goals
Real goals actually pursued. What org actually prioritizes in practice.
Primary vs. Secondary Goals
Primary Goals
Fundamental purpose (profit, social service, survival)
Secondary Goals
Means to achieve primary goals or stakeholder benefits
Goal Pathologies
Goal Displacement: Means become ends. Following procedures becomes goal instead of achieving purpose (bureaucratic pathology)
Goal Succession: Original goal achieved, organization pursues new goals to justify continued existence (mission drift)

Topic 02 / Page 2
Goal Pathologies (cont.)
⚠️ Organisational Dysfunctions
More Goal Pathologies
Ritualism: Rigid adherence to rules and procedures. Focus on form over substance. "We follow the rules" becomes the goal itself.
Sub-goal Fixation: Departments obsess over sub-goals and lose sight of organizational objectives. Marketing prioritizes market share over profit; Finance cuts costs harming quality.
Goal Clarity Framework
Mission (Why org exists)
↓
Vision (Desired future state)
↓
Strategic Goals (3-5 years)
↓
Operational Objectives (Annual)
🇮🇳 Indian Context: Many organizations struggle with goal clarity due to multiple stakeholder pressures (government, unions, communities). Clear cascading goals help navigate these competing demands.

Topic 03 / Page 1
Evolution of Theory
📈 Classical School (1900-1930)
Key Contributors
Taylor (Scientific Management): Standardize tasks, use time-motion studies, differential piece-rate pay. Goal: maximize efficiency through rational analysis.
Fayol (Administrative Principles): 14 principles including unity of command, scalar chain, division of labor. Functions: planning, organizing, directing, controlling.
Weber (Ideal Bureaucracy): Hierarchical authority, written rules, role-based positions, merit-based promotion. Rational-legal legitimacy.
Classical Assumptions
One best way to organize work
People are rational economic actors
Motivation purely financial (wages)
Managers plan; workers execute
Formal structure = actual behavior

Topic 03 / Page 2
Neo-Classical & Modern
👥 Neo-Classical & Systems
Neo-Classical School (1930-1950)
Hawthorne Studies (Mayo): Discovered informal groups and social needs matter more than financial incentives. Productivity increased due to attention and group dynamics, not just money.
Informal organization exists alongside formal structure
Social man (not just economic man) drives motivation
Group norms override individual incentives
Leadership style and participation matter
Modern Approaches
Systems View
Organization as open system with inputs, processes, outputs, feedback. Interacts with environment.
Contingency
No one best way. Design depends on situational variables (environment, technology, size, strategy).
🇮🇳 Evolution in India: Indian orgs moved from classical hierarchy to recognizing informal networks, family structures, and community relationships as key organizational factors.

Management by Objectives
📊 MBO Process (Drucker)
5-Step MBO Process
Step 1: Goal Setting (Manager sets organizational goals)
↓
Step 2: Cascading Objectives (Goals cascade down hierarchy)
↓
Step 3: Joint Participation (Employee & manager agree on objectives)
↓
Step 4: Periodic Review (Regular feedback on progress)
↓
Step 5: Final Appraisal (Performance evaluated against goals)
Key Feature: Participation
Core Principle: Employees participate in setting their own objectives, not just receiving top-down targets. Increases motivation and commitment.
Clear performance expectations
Regular feedback and adjustment
Alignment between individual and org goals

Topic 04 / Page 2
MBO Variants & OKRs
⭐ Modern Evolution
MBO: Strengths & Weaknesses
Strengths
Clear goals, motivation, focus, accountability, regular feedback
Weaknesses
Paperwork-heavy, short-term focus, difficult goals to quantify, inflexible
Modern Variant: OKRs
Objectives & Key Results
Objectives: Qualitative, inspirational goals ("Be the most innovative company")
Key Results: 3-4 measurable outcomes ("Launch 5 new products", "Achieve 4.8 customer rating")

Cadence: Quarterly (vs. annual for MBO)
Stretch: Aim for 70% achievement (vs. 100% in MBO)
Transparency: All OKRs visible across organization
🇮🇧 Indian Adoption: Many Indian tech startups and progressive PSUs are moving from MBO to OKRs for greater agility and quarterly adaptation to market changes.

Topic 05 / Page 1
Organisational Design
⚙️ Basic Design Challenges
Core Tensions in Design
Differentiation vs. Integration: Create specialized units for efficiency vs. need to coordinate them for effectiveness
Centralisation vs. Decentralisation: Top-down control and consistency vs. autonomy and local responsiveness
Standardisation vs. Mutual Adjustment: Standardized procedures for consistency vs. informal coordination for flexibility
Formal vs. Informal Coordination: Official hierarchies and processes vs. informal networks and relationships that actually get work done
Design Principles
Span of Control: How many subordinates can one manager supervise?
Chain of Command: Clear reporting relationships minimize confusion
Unity of Command: Each person reports to one superior
Delegation: Push authority and responsibility down

Topic 05 / Page 2
Structure Types
🏗️ Structural Configurations
Functional Structure
Focus: Expertise — Grouped by function (HR, Finance, Operations, Marketing). Efficient for standardized work. Problem: Silos and slow cross-functional coordination.
Divisional Structure (M-Form)
Focus: Output — Divided by product line, geography, or customer. Self-contained profit centers. Problem: Duplication of functions, higher costs.
Matrix Structure
Focus: Dual Authority — Employees report to both functional and project managers. Balances specialization with project focus. Problem: Complexity, ambiguity, multiple bosses.
Network Structure
Focus: Virtual/Fuzzy Boundaries — Decentralized, connected through IT and relationships. Flexible and innovative. Problem: Loss of control, coordination challenges.
🇮🇳 Common in India: Most PSUs use functional structures; IT companies use matrix; startups use network. Family businesses often blend divisional with centralized family control.

Topic 06 / Page 2
Span of Control & Conflict
🎯 Graicunas & Tensions
Graicunas: Span of Control Formula
Theory: As subordinates increase, manager's workload increases exponentially (not linearly). With 5 subordinates, 125 possible relationships. Optimal span is 4-6 people.
Factors affecting span: Complexity of task, manager skill, subordinate training, distance
Narrow span: More levels, slower decisions, costly
Wide span: Flatter structure, faster decisions, but overload risk
Line vs. Staff Conflict
Line Authority
Direct authority over operations. Makes final decisions. HR, Finance, Operations.
Staff Authority
Advisory, specialized expertise. No direct decision power. Consultants, analysts.
Line vs. Staff Tensions
Typical Conflict: Line resents staff "interference"; Staff feels ignored. Staff lacks authority to implement recommendations. Line blames staff when things go wrong.
Resolution: Clear authority boundaries, joint goal-setting, staff embedded in line units
🇮🇳 In Indian Orgs: PSUs often have bloated staff structures; family businesses have minimal staff; IT companies using cross-functional teams blur line/staff distinctions.

Organisational Culture
🎭 Schein's 3-Level Model
Level 1: Artifacts (Visible)
What you see & hear: Office design, dress code, language, symbols, logos, ceremonies, rituals, stories, legends, heroes. Easiest to observe but hardest to interpret.
Example: Open office layout signals collaboration and transparency
Example: Formal dress code signals conservatism and hierarchy
Level 2: Espoused Values (Conscious Beliefs)
What org says it values: Mission statements, codes of conduct. "We value integrity, innovation, teamwork, customer-focus." Often aspirational but may not match reality.
Level 3: Basic Assumptions (Unconscious Truths)
What org truly believes: Deepest level. Shapes actual behavior. Often invisible even to members. May contradict espoused values. "People can't be trusted", "Only profit matters", "Hierarchy is natural".
🇮🇳 Indian Example: Family business espouses "merit-based promotion" but assumes family members are naturally more capable. Gap between levels creates cynicism.

Culture Sustenance & Types
🎯 Handy's Typologies
How Culture is Sustained
Selection
Hire people who fit the culture. Screen out misaligned values early.
Leadership
Leaders model cultural values through behavior. Leader behavior shapes culture more than words.
Socialisation
Onboarding and training to integrate new members into culture. Stories, rituals, mentorship.
Stories & Symbols
Organizational narratives, legends, and symbols embed and transmit cultural values.
Handy's 4 Culture Types (Greek Gods)
Power Culture (Zeus)
Centralized authority, fast decisions, entrepreneurial feel. Dominated by strong leader. Few rules. Found in small companies, entrepreneurial startups, trading firms.
Role Culture (Apollo)
Bureaucratic, stable, role-based hierarchy. Written procedures, predictable. Large organizations, government, traditional industries. Slow to adapt but reliable.
Task Culture (Athena)
Project-focused, matrix structure. Flexible, problem-solving, team-based. Media, IT, consulting. Adaptable but complex.
Person Culture (Dionysus)
Individual-centered, minimal hierarchy. Professionals gather to pursue individual excellence. Law firms, universities, research. Difficult to manage.
Culture vs. Climate
Aspect Culture Climate Depth Deep, enduring beliefs Surface perceptions Change Years to change Changes with leadership Nature Causal (drives behavior) Effect (result of culture)

Impact of Information Technology
💻 Digital Transformation
Flatter Hierarchies
IT Effect: Technology enables direct communication across organization. Middle-management layers become redundant for information flow. Span of control increases; hierarchy flattens.
Email/messaging bypass middle managers
Shared databases eliminate gatekeepers
Remote work decouples location from reporting structure
Remote Performance Monitoring
IT Effect: Technology enables real-time tracking of outputs, activities, keystrokes, location. New form of control without physical presence. Creates "surveillance capitalism" concerns.
Productivity monitoring software tracks worker activity
Output metrics replace presence as control mechanism
Privacy concerns and employee resistance rising
Platform Business Models
IT Effect: Digital platforms (Uber, Airbnb, Upwork) create organizations without traditional hierarchy. Algorithmic coordination replaces management. Gig workers, no employees, minimal bureaucracy.
Peer ratings replace manager evaluation
Algorithms match supply and demand
Cost reduction and flexibility; worker uncertainty increased
🇮🇧 Impact in India: IT companies pioneered flat structures; E-commerce disruptors using platform models; Traditional PSUs struggling with digital transformation and need for agility.
