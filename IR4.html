⚖️ ADJUDICATION - Concept, Three-Tier Machinery & Evaluation
IDA 1947 | Compulsory & Binding | Three Tiers | IR Code 2020 Reforms
ADJUDICATION
DEFINITION & LEGAL BASIS (Exam Critical)
Core Definition Settlement of industrial disputes through quasi-judicial process by statutory authority whose award is COMPULSORY & legally BINDING
Key Distinction COMPULSORY (govt-initiated) | BINDING (no choice to comply) | Third-party determination (unlike CB which is bipartite)
Legal Basis Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 (IDA) — Chapter V; Government issues reference order under Sec. 10 triggering machinery
Unique to India Most developed democracies rely primarily on CB/arbitration; India's heavy adjudication-dependence is a STRUCTURAL WEAKNESS
ADJUDICATION vs OTHER METHODS (Comparison Critical)
vs Conciliation Conciliation = voluntary, non-binding, tripartite; Adjudication = compulsory, binding, government-decided
vs Arbitration Arbitration = requires CONSENT of both parties, binding by agreement; Adjudication = NO consent needed, STATE-IMPOSED binding award
vs Collective Bargaining CB = bipartite, bilateral, voluntary, flexible, renegotiable; Adjudication = third-party, compulsory, inflexible, legally final
THREE-TIER MACHINERY (IDA, 1947)
TIER 1: Labour Court (Sec. 7) Single person (HC/District judge). Jurisdiction: Second Schedule (dismissal, suspension, retrenchment, standing orders). Award binding 1–3 years
TIER 2: Industrial Tribunal (Sec. 7A) Single person. Jurisdiction: ALL disputes (both Schedules) incl. wages, bonus, working conditions. MOST COMMONLY USED BODY
TIER 3: National Industrial Tribunal (Sec. 7B) Central Govt. constitutes for national importance/inter-state disputes (railways, banking, insurance). Award has NATIONWIDE force
KEY SECTIONS & PROCEDURE
Sec. 10 (Government Power) Government's discretion to refer disputes — triggers machinery. WIDE discretionary power; frequently criticized for delay & misuse
Sec. 17/17A (Award Publication) Award published in Official Gazette within 30 days; operative for 1 year unless govt. specifies otherwise
Sec. 18 (Binding Settlements) Binding nature of settlements & awards — covers ALL parties and ALL workers in establishment
Sec. 22/23 (Strike Prohibition) 14-day notice in public utilities; during pending proceedings before Tribunal/Court — prevents economic pressure during adjudication

EVALUATION
MERITS OF ADJUDICATION (5 Key Points)
1. Final & Binding No dispute remains permanently unresolved — award is final and must be complied with; provides certainty
2. Judicial Expertise Expert determination of complex IR disputes — quasi-judicial authority with industrial relations knowledge
3. Protects Weaker Party Protects workers from employer unilateralism — state intervention as checks on dominant employer power
4. Deterrent Effect Deters illegal strikes and lockouts; Sec. 22/23 strike prohibition creates pressure to resolve through adjudication
5. Uniformity of Standards Ensures consistent standards across establishments; prevents exploitative variations in working conditions
LIMITATIONS / CRITICISMS (6 Key Points - V.V. Giri Quote)
1. EXTREME DELAY (3–7 years average) From reference to award → unacceptable for workers who need immediate relief; justice delayed = justice denied
2. Government Discretion (Sec. 10) Political misuse: reluctance to refer sensitive disputes; selective application; undermines impartiality
3. Legalisation of IR Parties rely on lawyers, killing CB culture; dispute resolution becomes adversarial legal battle instead of negotiated settlement
4. Limited Expertise Single-person composition (Labour Court/Tribunal) lacks multi-disciplinary perspective; no management expert involved
5. Coverage Limitations Limited to organised 'workmen' in 'industry' as defined; vast majority of informal economy workers excluded
6. Enforcement Deficit Awards frequently challenged in HC/SC by employers; appeals delay implementation; compliance mechanisms weak
V.V. GIRI CRITIQUE (Must Quote in Exam)
V.V. Giri Quote (India's Labour Minister 1950s) "Adjudication should be the exception, not the rule."
Critical Point India's IR system became the INVERSE — adjudication-dependence is a SYMPTOM of underdeveloped collective bargaining & weak bipartite institutions
Implication Fix the root cause: strengthen CB, build union strength, improve labour court infrastructure, reduce Sec. 10 discretion
IR CODE 2020 — KEY REFORMS
Two-Member Industrial Tribunal Replaces single-member Labour Court + Tribunal — judicial member + administrative member. Greater balance; managerial expertise included
Negotiating Union (>51% membership) Sole bargaining agent — reduces disputes reaching adjudication by strengthening bipartite CB
Fixed-Term Employment (FTE) Codifies FTE contracts — clear statutory terms reduce disputes over termination & retrenchment
Worker Re-skilling Fund Employer contribution on retrenchment — links dispute resolution to labour market mobility & transition
Thresholds Raised Standing Orders (300+ from 100) | Chapter V-B permission (300+ from 100) — reduces compliance burden; major flexibility

💼 COLLECTIVE BARGAINING - Definition, Nature, Theories & Process
Flippo Definition | 5 Characteristics | 4 Theories | 6-Step Process | ILO Convention 98
COLLECTIVE BARGAINING
DEFINITIONS (Key Thinkers)
Edwin Flippo (MOST CITED) "CB is a process in which representatives of labour organisation and business organisation meet to negotiate a contract specifying employee-employer-union relationship"
ILO Convention 98 (1949) CB covers negotiations between employers/employers' organisations and workers' organisations to determine wages & conditions of employment
Dunlop & Healey CB creates the 'web of rules' governing employment — simultaneously a means of IR and a rulebook for the organisation
Core Result Collective Bargaining Agreement (CBA) / Settlement — legally binding under Sec. 18 of IDA 1947
5 CHARACTERISTICS / NATURE OF CB
1. Collective Process Union & management represent collective interests; outcome binds ALL workers in bargaining unit
2. Continuous Process NOT a one-time event — ongoing relationship; regular renegotiation (typically 2–3 year cycles)
3. Bipartite & Voluntary Bilateral — state acts only as facilitator/regulator. Genuine CB = no coercive state intervention
4. Flexible & Dynamic Unlike judicial awards, CB agreements can be renegotiated by mutual consent to adapt to change
5. Industrial Democracy Extends democratic principles to workplace — ILO characterises CB as a fundamental right
4 THEORIES OF CB (Exam Important)
Trade Union Theory (Commons, 1924) CB emerged as workers' response to power imbalance in individual bargaining. Unions restore bargaining parity
Marketing Theory (Chamberlain) Labour is a commodity; CB negotiates its price. Bilateral monopoly model — union monopolises supply, employer controls demand
Governmental Theory (Chamberlain) CB is workplace legislation — creates binding rules equivalent to public law. Union is co-legislator with management
Integrative/Principled Bargaining (Fisher & Ury, 1981) Interest-based negotiation — focus on underlying interests, multiple options, objective criteria. Produces mutually beneficial outcomes
6-STEP CB PROCESS
1. Preparation Both sides collect data: wage statistics, productivity, CPI, profitability. Union formulates demands; management prepares counter-position
2. Charter of Demands Union presents comprehensive list of demands. Management analyses and prepares response
3. Negotiations Back-and-forth exchange of proposals. Positional bargaining (compromise to middle) OR principled bargaining (Fisher & Ury)
4. Impasse Resolution Options: Conciliation officer | Voluntary arbitration | Industrial action (strike/lockout) | Govt. reference → adjudication
5. Settlement Terms reduced to writing — Sec. 18 settlement signed by authorised representatives. Binding on ALL parties and workers
6. Implementation Agreement implemented; grievance mechanism for disputes about interpretation or application of settlement terms

CB & PSU IR
CB CHALLENGES IN INDIA (6 + Remedies)
1. Union Fragmentation 10+ central federations (INTUC, AITUC, HMS, CITU, BMS) — Remedy: IR Code 2020 introduced negotiating union concept (>51%)
2. Low Union Density (~8% of total workforce) ILO 2023 data — Remedy: Check-off facility; e-registration; expand TU membership to informal sector
3. Adjudication-Dependence Judicial awards preferred over negotiations — Remedy: Reduce Sec. 10 discretion; incentivise bipartite settlements
4. Managerial Hostility EPZ/SEZ sectors resist union recognition & CB — Remedy: Enforce recognition provisions; ILO Convention 98 ratification
5. Political Unionism TU affiliations distort wage demands by electoral cycles — Remedy: Delink TUs from political parties; worker-member leadership
6. No National Recognition Law Historically absent — Remedy: IR Code 2020's negotiating union (>51%) — full implementation needed across states
PSU IR — UNIQUE FEATURES (Dual Role of State)
Dual Role of State (CRITICAL) Government is simultaneously OWNER + EMPLOYER + LEGISLATOR/ADJUDICATOR — creates structural conflicts of interest absent in private sector
Public Interest Dimension PSU operations (power, water, transport, banking) affect public directly — disruptions have national consequences; essential services restrictions apply
Political Unionism INTUC (Congress), BMS (BJP), CITU (CPM), AITUC (CPI) — all have strong PSU footholds; union activities politically driven
Job Security Paradox PSU workers enjoy higher security → weakens management bargaining leverage; no retrenchment without govt. approval
Wage Determination (NOT purely bilateral CB) Pay Revision Committees, Wage Boards (banking, coal), or bipartite settlements — structural difference from private sector
Performance Paradox (CRITICAL) High union power + govt. ownership → higher wages/security with no commercial pressure to resist → OVERSTAFFING & INEFFICIENCY
PSU DISPUTE SETTLEMENT & MECHANISMS
Bipartite Negotiation (First Resort) Direct PSU management + recognised union(s) negotiation. Most effective in central PSUs with strong single unions (SAIL-INTUC)
Conciliation (Mandatory) Conciliation officer facilitates before adjudication reference. Mandatory in public utility services
Pay Revision Committees (Periodic) Central PSUs: PRC every 10 years | Banking: Joint Bipartite Committee on Wages (IBA + unions) | Coal: JBCCI
ESMA (Essential Services Maintenance Act) Prohibits strikes in essential services for 6 months. Frequently invoked; weakens union power but creates resentment
Sec. 10 Reference (Last Resort) Government as PSU owner refers to Industrial Tribunal. Problem: delays; govt. reluctance to reveal poor HR policies
Joint Management Councils (Consultative) Regular consultation on production, welfare, grievances — statutory in some sectors; builds cooperative IR
PSU IR — STRENGTHS vs PROBLEMS
✓ Strengths Highest unionisation rate in India | Strong collective voice in wage revision | Effective banking bipartite model (IBA 12th BPS 2020) | Check on arbitrary management action
✗ Problems Multi-union fragmentation (SAIL Bhilai: 20+ unions) | Political affiliation distorts demands | 'Outsider leadership' — politicians, not worker-members | ESMA invocations weaken bargaining | Air India/transport: political interference → management paralysis
CONTRAST — SUCCESS vs FAILURE SUCCESS: Banking (IBA bipartite — professional, data-driven, 12th BPS 2020) | Coal (JBCCI) | Steel (Tata, SAIL LTA) | FAILURE: Air India (12+ unions, political interference → privatised 2022) | State Road Transport (perennial strikes)

ABSENTEEISM
DEFINITION & MEASUREMENT (Exam Critical)
Definition Habitual/frequent failure to report for work as scheduled WITHOUT valid justification. Excludes authorised leave (casual, medical, earned)
Formula Absenteeism Rate = (Man-Days Lost / Total Scheduled Man-Days) × 100
Benchmarks (India Context) Acceptable: 3–5% | High: >10% | India manufacturing average: 8–12% | Seasonal industries (jute, tea): up to 20%+ (NMCC 2021)
4 Types Voluntary (job dissatisfaction) | Involuntary (illness, emergency) | Culpable (no justification) | Non-culpable (genuine personal reasons)
CAUSES - 5 CATEGORIES (India-Specific)
Personal/Individual Ill-health, alcoholism, substance abuse, domestic problems, moonlighting. India-specific: Chronic occupational illness in mining/construction
Job-Related/Organisational Monotonous work, poor conditions, safety fears, long hours, night shifts, autocratic supervision. India-specific: Heat/dust/noise in factories; Factories Act gaps
Social & Cultural Festivals, religious observances, agricultural seasonality. India-specific: 'Monday absenteeism'; 'payday absenteeism'; harvest-season returns
Economic Inadequate wages → second jobs; poor transport/housing; no health insurance. India-specific: Long commutes from distant housing; no ESI in small factories
Psychological (Adams' Equity Theory) Industrial conflict, supervisor fear, low morale, burnout, perceived inequity. India-specific: Authoritarian management → workers fake illness rather than confront
EFFECTS (5 CONSEQUENCES)
Production Disruption Machine downtime; quality reduction when replacement workers substituted
Direct Costs Overtime wages; idle machinery; rescheduling costs
Indirect Costs Training replacements; customer service failures; reputational harm
Morale Damage Burden shifts to present workers → resentment → CHAIN ABSENTEEISM
QWL Signal (CRITICAL) High absenteeism = underlying Quality of Work Life dysfunction — management failure signal
REMEDIES / CONTROL MEASURES (5)
A. Administrative Biometric attendance; progressive discipline; return-to-work interviews
B. Health & Welfare Occupational health centres (Factories Act); EAP (Employee Assistance Programme — counselling for alcohol/mental health)
C. Welfare Facilities Subsidised transport, canteen, housing near workplace
D. Motivational (Hackman-Oldham Job Design) Attendance bonus; job enrichment; quality circles; skill variety, task identity, autonomy reduce boredom-driven absence
E. Cultural Schedule paid leave for major festivals; 'festival advance' schemes; flexible working; accommodate social/cultural observances

LABOUR TURNOVER
DEFINITION & MEASUREMENT (3 Formulas)
Turnover Rate (Separation) — Most Common (Number of Separations / Avg. Employees) × 100. India IT sector: 20–25%; Manufacturing: 10–15%; Banking: 5–8%
Accession Rate — Secondary (New Joinings / Avg. Employees) × 100. High accession + high separation = 'churning' — expensive & disruptive
Stability Index (Bowey) — Most Meaningful for HR (Employees with >1 year service / Total employees 1 year ago) × 100. Measures ORGANISATIONAL STABILITY, not just gross movement
Wastage Rate (NIIP Formula) — Research Use (Total Separations / Workforce at Beginning of Period) × 100. Used for longitudinal comparisons
4 TYPES OF LABOUR TURNOVER
1. Voluntary vs Involuntary Voluntary: Employee-initiated (resignation, better opportunity, retirement) — indicates dissatisfaction; MOST COSTLY | Involuntary: Employer-initiated (retrenchment, dismissal, VRS) — regulated by Sec. 25F IDA
2. Avoidable vs Unavoidable Avoidable: Preventable by better HR (low pay, poor conditions, bad management) — HIGHEST policy relevance | Unavoidable: Death, disability, migration, spouse's transfer — cannot prevent
3. Functional vs Dysfunctional Functional: Poor performers replaced by better ones — BENEFICIAL | Dysfunctional: Departure of high performers & specialists — MOST DAMAGING (acute in IT poaching & PSU-private pay gap)
CRITICAL DISTINCTION (Exam Important) Organisations should NOT aim for zero turnover — some turnover is healthy & functional; the goal is to REDUCE DYSFUNCTIONAL turnover (loss of skilled workers)
CAUSES (7 Categories — India Context)
1. Wage/Compensation Low wages vs market; PSU-private sector salary differential (e.g. PSU bank clerk ₹25k vs private ₹40k); no variable pay; IT boom creates poaching pressure
2. Working Conditions Unsafe conditions (India: high occupational accidents in mining, construction, chemicals); night shifts; long hours
3. Job Dissatisfaction & Career Limited promotion; no training; monotony; poor performance management; lack of autonomy
4. Management/IR Climate Autocratic management; victimisation of union activists; excessive contract labour; perceived favouritism
5. Personal/Social Marriage migration (women in garment, textile); seasonal agricultural pull; family obligations
6. Economic/Structural IT sector boom; gig economy flexible alternatives; urbanisation increasing worker mobility & aspirations
7. NASSCOM Data (IT Sector) Replacing one IT professional costs 6–9 months of their salary
TURNOVER COSTS & REMEDIES (6-Point Strategy)
Cost Framework: DIRECT + TRAINING + INDIRECT Direct: Exit administration, severance pay, recruitment, assessment | Training: Induction + on-job + mentoring (new employee ~50% productivity for 3-6 months) | Indirect: Knowledge loss, team morale contagion, management time diversion, 'difficult employer' brand
1. Recruitment & Selection Structured interviews, realistic job previews, psychometric testing — better person-job fit reduces early turnover
2. Compensation & Benefits Market-competitive wages; performance-linked pay; health insurance, PF, gratuity, housing loan; transparent pay progression
3. Career Development Clear career paths; structured training; mentoring; merit-based promotion; job rotation for skill development
4. Working Conditions Factories Act compliance; ergonomics; flexible scheduling; remote work; reduce excessive overtime
5. Management Quality Supervisor training; 360° feedback; employee engagement surveys; exit interview analysis
6. Retention Policies Service bonds for trained employees; loyalty bonuses; sabbatical; flexible retirement; women return-to-work programmes
