Transactional Analysis (TA)

👤 EGO STATES
TRANSACTIONAL ANALYSIS
Three Core Ego States
Parent (Critical & Nurturing)
Introjected: Internalized voices of parents/authority figures. Contains values, rules, shoulds and shouldn'ts
Critical Parent: Judgmental, fault-finding, controlling
Nurturing Parent: Caring, supportive, protective
Adult (Rational & Objective)
Logical, data-driven, problem-solving state. Processes information objectively without emotional bias. Makes rational decisions based on facts. The mature, responsible part of personality. Best for decision-making and conflict resolution
Child (Natural & Adapted)
Natural Child: Spontaneous, creative, playful, emotional, intuitive, rebellious
Adapted Child: Learned to comply, conform, suppress natural impulses. "Good behavior," follows rules
Healthy Balance
Effective people use all three ego states situationally. Parent for guidance, Adult for problem-solving, Child for creativity. Problems arise when stuck in one state
🔄 TRANSACTION TYPES
How Ego States Interact
Complementary Transactions
Parallel flow between ego states. Person 1 addresses ego state, Person 2 responds from that same state. Communication flows smoothly. "I have a problem" (Adult) → "Let me help" (Adult). No conflict
Crossed Transactions
Lines cross. Expected response doesn't match actual response. Breakdown in communication. "Why are you so careless?" (Critical Parent) → "I'm not careless!" (Natural Child). Results in conflict and misunderstanding
Ulterior (Hidden) Transactions
Hidden psychological message beneath social message. "Why don't you come to my place for coffee?" (Adult on surface, seductive Child underneath). Games played where real message is different from stated message
✅ LIFE POSITIONS (I'M OK, YOU'RE OK)
Four Basic Positions
I'm OK, You're OK (Ideal)
Healthy, balanced position. I accept myself and respect others. Mutual respect and collaboration. Open to feedback. Trusting. Builds strong relationships and effective organizations
I'm OK, You're Not OK (Arrogant)
Superiority position. "I'm right, you're wrong." Dismissive of others' views. Authoritarian, controlling, blames others for problems. Creates resentment and conflict
I'm Not OK, You're OK (Inferior)
Subordinate position. "You're better than me." Seeks approval from others. Low self-esteem, dependent. Avoids decisions, always blames self. Creates learned helplessness
I'm Not OK, You're Not OK (Nihilistic)
Hopeless, defeated position. "Nothing matters." Gives up on self and others. Pessimistic, cynical, withdrawn. Most destructive. Associated with depression and burnout
🪟 JOHARI WINDOW & STROKES
Self-Awareness for Better Communication
Johari Window Quadrants
Open Known to self and others
Blind Unknown to self, known to others
Hidden Known to self, hidden from others
Unknown Unknown to both
Strokes: Units of Recognition
Smallest units of human recognition/attention. Positive strokes (praise, attention) build esteem. Negative strokes better than no strokes. Unconditional strokes value person; conditional value actions. Critical for human motivation

Organisational Effectiveness (OE)

📊 ORGANISATIONAL EFFECTIVENESS MODELS
MEASURING EFFECTIVENESS
Five Major Models
Goal Attainment Model
Effectiveness = extent organization achieves its stated goals and objectives. Simple, intuitive. Limitation: not all important outcomes are formal goals. Gaming/goal displacement risk
Systems Resource Model
Effectiveness = ability to acquire and manage resources. Inputs (capital, talent), processes, outputs. Organization effective if it secures resources and uses them efficiently. Resource-centric view
Internal Process Model
Effectiveness = smooth, efficient internal functioning. Communication, coordination, employee satisfaction, low conflict. Focus on organizational health and harmony. Culture and relationships matter
Strategic Constituencies Model
Effectiveness = satisfying all stakeholders (employees, customers, shareholders, community). Balanced approach. Must please diverse groups. Acknowledges multiple perspectives of effectiveness
Quinn's Competing Values Framework
Four competing models: Rational goal (productivity), internal process (stability), human relations (morale), open systems (innovation). Effective orgs balance all four. No single best model
✨ POSITIVE PSYCHOLOGY & OE
PERMA Model (Seligman)
P - Positive Emotions
Happiness, contentment, joy in work. Contribute to engagement and performance. Optimistic work environment builds morale
E - Engagement
Absorption in work, flow state, focus. Time flies when engaged. Peak performance happens in engagement. Work feels meaningful and challenging
R - Relationships
Quality connections, supportive colleagues, trust. Strong relationships are core human need. Communities thrive on relationships
M - Meaning
Purpose, contribution to something larger. Work connected to values. Meaning drives commitment and motivation. Why we do work matters
A - Accomplishment
Achievement, progress, mastery. Goals reached, competence felt. Sense of getting better and advancing
Psychological Capital (HERO)
Hope: Goal-directed energy. I can find path to success
Efficacy: Confidence in ability to succeed. I can do this
Resilience: Bounce back from setbacks. I can overcome challenges
Optimism: Positive outlook about future. Good things will happen
Flow & OCB
Flow States (Csikszentmihalyi)
Condition: Challenge-skill balance. Neither bored nor anxious. Complete absorption. Peak performance and satisfaction. Organizations should design work for flow
Organisational Citizenship Behaviour
Voluntary, discretionary helping behavior. Go beyond job requirements. Assist colleagues, organizational loyalty, positive attitude. Sign of genuine engagement and positive psychology
🔄 SOB-C MODEL
Stimulus–Organism–Behaviour–Consequence
S → O → B → C Framework: Stimulus triggers individual (organism) responses based on perception, values, beliefs, leading to behavior, which has consequences. Understanding this chain enables better management and prediction

Management of Change

📈 CORE CHANGE MANAGEMENT MODELS
MANAGING CHANGE
Classic & Contemporary Models
Lewin's 3-Step Model
1. Unfreeze: Create awareness need for change. Challenge status quo, increase dissatisfaction with current state
2. Move: Transition period. New behaviors, learning, experimentation
3. Refreeze: Stabilize new state. Reinforce, make permanent
Force Field Analysis
Identifies driving forces (push change forward) and restraining forces (resist change). Increase driving forces or decrease restraining forces. Understand opposition to manage it
Kotter's 8-Step Model
Create urgency Build coalition Form vision Communicate Empower action Create wins Consolidate Embed culture
Adaptive Coping Cycle
From Sense (notice change needed) → Interpret (understand implications) → Decide (what to do) → Act (implement) → Evaluate (assess results) → Continuous learning cycle
⚠️ RESISTANCE TO CHANGE
Individual Factors
Habit & Comfort
Comfort with current state. Change requires energy and learning. Easier to continue doing familiar things. Inertia is powerful force against change
Insecurity & Fear
Fear of losing job, skills becoming obsolete, unable to learn new ways. Uncertainty about future. Anxiety about competence in new environment
Misperception
Misunderstanding change rationale. Hearing rumors instead of facts. Assuming worst-case scenarios. Poor communication amplifies perception problems
Organisational Factors
Structural Inertia
Systems, procedures, rules designed for old way. Slow to change even when intent is there. Bureaucracy resists change. Policies reinforce status quo
Power Threats
Those losing power resist change. Hierarchy threatened by empowerment. Middle managers may resist if change reduces their authority or scope
Overcoming Resistance
Education & Communication: Share rationale, benefits, plans. Reduce rumors and misperceptions
Participation & Involvement: Include people in planning. Ownership increases support
Support & Training: Build capability for new ways. Reduce fear through preparation
Incentives & Rewards: Reinforce new behaviors. Show early wins
🇮🇳 CHANGE MANAGEMENT IN INDIAN CONTEXT
Major Change Initiatives
Digital India & GeM
Digital transformation initiatives. Government e-Marketplace (GeM) digitizing procurement. Significant organizational and process changes. Requires technology adoption and new ways of working
Mission Karmayogi
Civil service reform. Competency-based approach replacing seniority. New appraisal systems (APAR reform). Significant cultural shift needed from traditional hierarchical system
PSU Restructuring & Disinvestment
Government restructuring public sector units. Mergers, privatization, strategic selling. Massive changes in employment, organizational structure, corporate culture
Challenges in Indian Context
High power distance makes change messaging difficult. Hierarchical structure slow to adapt. Union opposition to changes affecting workers. Public sector change faces bureaucratic inertia. Need to balance change with political pressures and cultural sensitivity
